Overview

Straddling the banks of the Rhine, Düsseldorf is a city of sharp contrasts. It is widely known as a premium German destination for luxury shopping and contemporary architecture, yet it retains a famously raucous spirit within its historic center. Sensory highlights include the yeasty scent of dark Altbier pouring in crowded taverns and the striking views of modern glass curves reflecting off the river at dusk.

Best time to visit: May to September. Most people spend 2 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 4 days with day trips to Cologne and Essen. Pack comfortable walking shoes for cobblestones and a light, rain-resistant jacket for sudden changes in weather.

Before you book

Check the city trade fair schedule (Messe Düsseldorf). Major international conventions can cause hotel prices to triple overnight and completely exhaust room availability weeks in advance.

Closed on certain days

Most retail shops on the Königsallee and elsewhere are strictly closed on Sundays. Museums almost universally close on Mondays, while traditional breweries remain open daily but get packed early on weekends.

Accessibility

Most U-Bahn stations have step-free elevator access. The historic Altstadt has uneven, historic cobblestones that can make wheelchair navigation challenging, though the Rhine Promenade is entirely smooth and accessible.

Common scams to know

Watch out for unlicensed drivers at the airport charging exorbitant flat rates. In the Altstadt, beware of aggressive pickpockets who look to distract you in crowded bar doorways.

Attractions - the full list

The unmissable four

Altstadt (Old Town)

Altstadt

Known as the longest bar in the world, this compact neighborhood packs over 250 bars, traditional breweries, and cafes into narrow alleys. It is the historic core of the city, where iconic dark copper-colored Altbier has been brewed and poured for centuries.

Free2-3 hours10:00 - 01:00Open daily
Tip: Don't wait to be served at a table; stand outside by the high barrels to drink like a true local and get faster service.

MedienHafen

Unterbilk

The old city harbor transformed into a showcase of modern architecture, famous for Frank Gehry's iconic, asymmetrical leaning office buildings. It is a striking contrast to the Altstadt, populated by design agencies, upscale restaurants, and fashion showrooms.

Free1-2 hours24 hoursOpen daily
Tip: Visit during the golden hour just before sunset to watch the shifting light reflect brilliantly off the metallic surfaces of the Gehry structures.

Rheinturm (Rhine Tower)

Unterbilk

Standing at 240 meters, this telecommunications tower provides the definitive panoramic view of the city and the sweeping Rhine curve. It features a revolving restaurant and a viewing platform, alongside a unique light installation that functions as the world's largest digital clock.

€12.501 hour10:00 - 23:00Open daily
Tip: Book a late afternoon slot to catch both daylight clarity and the spectacular city lights turning on across the river network below.

Schloss Benrath

Benrath

A striking 18th-century Baroque pleasure palace flanked by extensive, manicured formal gardens and a large lake. The pink-hued exterior leads into meticulously preserved neoclassical interiors that showcase the aristocratic luxury of the Elector Palatine Charles Theodore.

€14.002-3 hoursBest in morning or afternoon
Tip: The palace interior can only be viewed via guided tours; secure your slot online well in advance on weekends.

Walking

Düsseldorf is highly compact and largely flat. You can easily walk from the central train station through Little Tokyo to the Altstadt and down the Rhine promenade without needing transit, provided you have comfortable footwear for occasional cobblestones.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Düsseldorf combines dense traditional Rhineland tavern culture with Europe's most vibrant and authentic Japanese culinary hub.

The Great Altbier Debate

Locals fiercely debate which traditional old-town brewery serves the definitive Altbier. The choices range from the malty depth of Uerige to the slightly smoother profiles found at Füchschen or Schumacher, and asking for a different brand inside any brewery is considered an absolute faux pas.

Coffee/drink culture

Ordering coffee is straightforward, but beer follows a strict custom: in traditional breweries, waiters (Köbes) will continuously replace your empty glass of Altbier with a fresh one, marking your cardboard coaster with a pencil stroke, until you place the coaster on top of your glass to signal you are finished.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Düsseldorf rewards photographers who look for clean geometric lines and classic historic facades juxtaposed against dramatic river elements.

  • The Gehry BuildingsShoot from across the harbor basin bridge to frame the warped metallic reflections clearly.
  • Königsallee CanalStand on the central Triton Fountain bridge in the morning for crisp water reflections.
  • Burgplatz & SchlossturmCapture from the lower steps of the promenade at twilight to catch the warm lit tower glow.
  • Kiefernstraße MuralsWalk down this street during overcast days for vibrant, even colors on the massive wall graffiti.
  • Rheinturm Observation DeckPress your camera lens directly against the slanted glass to eliminate indoor light reflections.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Always carry some physical euro cash because many traditional breweries and small sausage stalls still refuse to accept credit cards.
  • Never step foot into the designated red asphalt bike lanes along the Rhine promenade unless you want a loud scolding from local cyclists.
  • If you visit during late May, do not miss Japan Day, an incredible celebration that turns the entire river bank into a lively festival of food, cosplay, and fireworks.
  • Take full advantage of the clean, safe public transit network at night instead of relying on expensive taxi ranks.
  • Do not throw away plastic water or beer bottles; look for the "Pfand" logo to return them to grocery machines for a cash refund.
  • Keep an eye on the side of the Rhine Tower at night to see if you can decode the vertical flashing dots that display the time in a binary format.
