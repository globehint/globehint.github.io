Start here

Overview

Lisbon rewards people who walk slowly and get a little lost. The city is built across seven hills, which is charming until your second day, at which point it is simply a workout. Expect tiled buildings, fado music drifting out of tiny restaurants, and a light that photographers fly in specifically to see.

Best time to visit: April–June or September–October, when it's warm without the August crowds and 35°C heat. Most people spend 3–4 days, though you could comfortably stretch to a week with day trips to Sintra and Cascais.

Before you book

Lisbon's airport sits inside the city, which is unusually convenient — you can be checking into a hotel within 30 minutes of landing. Keep this in mind when comparing it to cities where the airport transfer eats half a day.

Attractions — the full list

The unmissable four

Belém Tower

Belém

A 16th-century fortified tower built to guard the mouth of the Tagus, and one of the most photographed buildings in Portugal. The Manueline stonework on the outside is the real draw — the inside is fairly bare, so don't expect a long visit once you're in.

€6 entry30–45 min visitOpen 9:30am–6:30pmClosed Mondays
Tip: Arrive right at 9:30am opening. By 11am the queue regularly hits an hour, and there's almost no shade while you wait. Combine tickets with Jerónimos Monastery to save a few euros.

Jerónimos Monastery

Belém

The single most jaw-dropping building in Lisbon. Built to commemorate Vasco da Gama's voyage, the cloisters are an extraordinary, almost lace-like example of Manueline architecture — it's worth the entry fee for the cloisters alone, even if you skip the church.

€10 entry (cloisters)Church free~1 hr visitClosed Mondays
Tip: Book timed-entry tickets online a day ahead — it completely skips the line, which can otherwise run 45+ minutes in peak season.

Alfama district

Alfama

Lisbon's oldest neighbourhood and the one part of the city that survived the 1755 earthquake largely intact. Steep, narrow lanes, laundry strung between balconies, and fado music drifting from tiny restaurants at night. This isn't a single attraction so much as a place to lose an afternoon — see our self-guided walking tour further down this page.

Free to wanderBest: morning or eveningHalf day minimum
Tip: Go uphill first thing in the morning before the heat, then let gravity bring you back down in the afternoon.

Miradouro da Senhora do Monte

Graça

The single best free view in Lisbon, looking out over the castle, the river, and the rooftops of the old town. It's a five-minute walk further uphill than the more famous (and more crowded) Miradouro da Graça — most tourists stop too early.

Free~20 minBest at sunset
Tip: Bring a bottle of wine from a nearby kiosk — it's a totally normal, low-key local thing to do here at golden hour.

Also genuinely worth your time

São Jorge Castle

Alfama

Moorish-era hilltop castle with the best panoramic views in the city. Pricier than the miradouros nearby, but you're paying for the elevated walkways and peacocks roaming the grounds.

€15 entry1.5 hrsOpen daily 9am–9pm (summer)

LX Factory

Alcântara

A former industrial complex turned into bookshops, indie boutiques, murals, and a rooftop bar. Best on a Sunday when the flea market stalls are out.

Free to enter1–2 hrsLivelier on weekends

National Azulejo (Tile) Museum

Xabregas

A slightly out-of-the-way museum, but a genuinely rewarding deep dive into the tilework that covers half the city's buildings. Quiet even in peak season.

€5 entry1 hrClosed Mondays

Time Out Market

Cais do Sodré

A food hall packed with stalls from well-known local chefs. Touristy and busy at peak lunch hours, but a genuinely efficient way to sample a lot of Lisbon's food scene in one sitting.

Free entryPay per stallBest before 12pm or after 3pm

Skip unless you're a completionist

The Lisbon Story Centre and most paid "experience" museums near Praça do Comércio are overpriced for what's essentially a 20-minute video with limited educational value.

Getting around the city

A single rechargeable card called the Viva Viagem covers metro, trams, buses, and the funiculars — get one on day one.

Tram 28

The famous yellow tram through Alfama is more tourist attraction than transit at this point. Ride it once for the experience, ideally before 8:30am, but don't rely on it to actually get anywhere on time.

Walking

Lisbon's center is compact and very walkable — but the hills are real. Comfortable shoes aren't optional, they're required gear.

Metro

Four lines, clean, and the easiest way to cover longer distances. Closes around 1am.

Where to eat & drink

Beyond the obvious pastel de nata photo-op, here's where locals actually eat.

For pastel de nata (the real debate)

Pastéis de Belém is the famous original and worth the queue once. But ask five locals and at least two will say Manteigaria, near Time Out Market, is just as good with zero wait.

Coffee culture

Order a "bica" — that's a Lisbon espresso. Asking for a "café" works too, but ordering like a local gets a smile from most baristas.

Good to know

Portuguese is not pronounced like Spanish, even though it looks similar written down — the sounds are softer and more nasal. Don't be surprised if a Spanish phrase gets a polite blank look.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • The hills are no joke — pack proper walking shoes, not sandals.
  • Tipping isn't obligatory like in the US; rounding up or 5–10% is plenty.
  • Many small restaurants are cash-preferred, especially in Alfama — carry some euros.
  • Sunday mornings are quiet almost everywhere except the Feira da Ladra flea market.
  • July and August are very hot (30°C+) and very crowded — shoulder season is genuinely better.
  • Water fountains around the city are drinkable — refill a bottle instead of buying more plastic.
