Overview

Munich effortlessly matches cultural gravitas with an undeniably relaxed pace of life. Behind its grand neoclassical facades and impeccably clean avenues lies a city that values outdoor relaxation, Alpine views, and historic tavern culture above all else. Whether you are strolling past world-class galleries or sitting under chestnut trees with a cold liter of Helles, Munich shows you how to live well without rushing.

Best time to visit: May to September for the full beer garden experience, or December for the atmospheric Christmas markets. Most people spend 3 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 5 days with day trips to Neuschwanstein Castle and Salzburg. Pack comfortable walking shoes for cobblestones and a light jacket, as Alpine breezes can cool down summer evenings rapidly.

Before you book

If you are planning to visit during Oktoberfest (late September to early October), you must book accommodation and tavern tables up to 6-12 months in advance. Hotel rates triple during this window, so avoid these dates entirely if you are not looking to participate in the festival.

Closed on certain days

Most retail stores, boutiques, and supermarkets throughout Munich are strictly closed on Sundays due to federal law. Many independent restaurants close on Mondays, and major museums offer a reduced ticket entry fee on Sundays, which makes them crowded.

Accessibility

Munich's U-Bahn and S-Bahn stations are highly accessible with step-free lifts, but the historic Altstadt features patches of heavy, uneven cobblestones that can challenge manual wheelchairs.

Common scams to know

Watch out for unlicensed taxis offering flat rates outside the Hauptbahnhof, and pickpockets operating around the packed Marienplatz square during the daily Glockenspiel show.

The unmissable four

Marienplatz & Neues Rathaus

Altstadt

The historical and geographic heart of Munich, dominated by the staggering Neo-Gothic New Town Hall. It is home to the famous Glockenspiel clock show, which features mechanical figures performing dances and tournaments from Bavarian history.

Free1 hourExterior open 24/7None
Tip: The clock show at 11am and 12pm draws huge crowds; skip watching the slow mechanical dance at ground level and instead take the elevator up the Rathaus tower for a magnificent panorama.

Englischer Garten

Schwabing

One of the world's largest urban parks, stretching along the Isar River. It is a sprawling oasis featuring rolling lawns, a wooden Chinese Tower beer garden, and the iconic Eisbachwelle, an artificial wave where river surfers ride year-round.

Free3 hoursOpen 24/7None
Tip: Walk down to the southern tip at Prinzregentenstrasse to watch the skilled surfers tackle the river wave; it is one of Munich's most unique spectacles.

Munich Residenz

Altstadt

The vast former royal palace of the Wittelsbach monarchs. This magnificent complex contains dozens of highly decorated rooms, architectural monuments, and the striking Antiquarium, a majestic Renaissance hall lined with classical antiquities.

€10 - €152-3 hours9am - 6pmNone
Tip: Buy the combined ticket that includes the exquisite Cuvilliés Theatre, a pocket-sized, jaw-dropping Rococo opera house hidden inside the palace walls.

Nymphenburg Palace

Neuhausen

A grand, sweeping Baroque palace that served as the primary summer residence for Bavarian kings. It features lavishly decorated interiors, a famous portrait gallery of beauties, and massive formal French gardens sliced by scenic canals.

€10 - €123 hoursMorning to late afternoon
Tip: Rent a traditional wooden gondola ride on the central canal during the summer for an exceptionally romantic perspective of the palace grounds.

Also genuinely worth your time

Deutsches Museum

Museumsinsel

The world's largest museum of science and technology, situated on an island in the Isar River. An absolute paradise for tech lovers with massive aerospace and maritime halls.

Maxvorstadt

One of the oldest galleries in the world, holding an extraordinary collection of European masterpieces from the Middle Ages through the Rococo period, including works by Rubens and Rembrandt.

A bustling, daily open-air food market operating since the early 19th century. It is packed with gourmet stands, local cheese mongers, florists, and a central beer garden.

Budget note

The area directly around the Hauptbahnhof (Central Station) offers the cheapest hotels and hostels in the city center. While highly convenient for trains and generally safe, parts of it can feel gritty and uninspiring at night compared to adjacent neighborhoods.

Move

Getting around the city

Munich operates a unified transit network (MVV) encompassing the U-Bahn (metro), S-Bahn (suburban rail), trams, and buses. Tickets must be validated in the stamping machines before boarding if they don't have a specific printed time.

Walking

The entire central core from Karlsplatz to Marienplatz is a completely pedestrianized zone. Walking is highly pleasurable, fast, and straightforward, though you should wear sturdy footwear to handle the expansive historic squares.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Bavarian cuisine is rich, hearty, and fundamentally designed to accompany great beer. Expect plenty of pork, dumplings, and massive pretzels.

The Great Weißwurst Debate

The definitive local specialty is Weißwurst, a delicate minced veal and pork sausage flavored with parsley and lemon. Tradition dictates that these sausages must never hear the noon bells chime. They are eaten strictly as a late-morning snack, freshly boiled, peeled out of their skins (zuizeln), and accompanied by sweet mustard, a fresh pretzel, and a Hefeweizen beer.

Coffee/drink culture

While third-wave espresso shops are thriving in Maxvorstadt, Munich's true beverage custom belongs to its historic breweries. Beer is served predominantly in half-liter or full-liter glass mugs (Maß). If you prefer something lighter, order a Radler, which is a crisp fifty-fifty blend of Helles lager and sparkling lemonade.

The U-Bahn metro lines run 24 hours a day on Friday and Saturday nights, making getting home safe and cheap. On weeknights, metro services pause around 1am, requiring you to flag down a night bus or an Uber.

Good to know

When clinking glasses in Munich's tavern or beer garden culture, it is vital to make direct eye contact with your companions and exclaim "Prost!" failing to maintain eye contact is jokingly said to bring bad luck.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Always keep cash on hand. While card usage increased recently, smaller beer gardens and local market stands still strictly decline plastic.
  • You can bring your own food to any traditional Munich beer garden. As long as you purchase drinks from the tavern counter, sitting with your own picnic is totally legal.
  • Be highly alert near red-painted lanes on pavements. These are strict bike paths, and distracted pedestrians walking in them will face aggressive bell-ringing from local cyclists.
  • Do not attempt to cross streets when the pedestrian signal is red. Jaywalking is highly frowned upon culturally, and police frequently issue on-the-spot fines.
  • Return your empty bottles or glasses to the counter. Most beer gardens include a deposit fee (Pfand) in the initial drink price, which is refunded upon return.
  • Visit the world-class art galleries on a Sunday. Admission to major institutions like the Alte Pinakothek drops to just €1 for the entire day.
