Overview

Perched high above the Salzkammergut region, Dachstein Krippenstein offers an alpine counter-narrative to Hallstatt's fairy-tale lakeside charm. It is a world of massive, jagged karst landscapes, massive underground caverns, and expansive views that reach deep into the Austrian Alps. The air is crisp, the scale is humbling, and the shifting mountain weather ensures that no two visits feel identical.

Best time to visit: May to October for summer operations. A full day is enough to see the highlights - aim to leave Hallstatt by 8:15am to catch the early cable car up. Essential packing note: Even on hot summer days, the ice caves sit below freezing, so a heavy fleece or windproof jacket and thick-soled hiking boots are non-negotiable.

Day trip or overnight?

A single day trip covers the main viewpoints, hiking loops, and cave networks comfortably. However, spending a night at one of the rustic high-altitude mountain lodges allows you to experience spectacular alpine sunrises and sunsets long after the last tourist cable car has descended.

Before you go

When you arrive at the valley station, you must check in at the cave ticket counter immediately to assign yourself a specific timed slot for the guided tour. Do not hike up to the caves first without getting your tour number stamped down below.

Closed on certain days

The cable car and cave attractions run daily throughout the summer season (typically May 1 to November 1). However, individual features like the Ice Cave and Mammut Cave are fully closed during the winter months, meaning off-season trips are strictly limited to skiing and winter snowshoeing.

Accessibility

The cable car cabins are wheelchair-accessible, but the terrain on the mountain is highly challenging. The trails to the caves and the Five Fingers platform involve steep, paved, or gravel inclines that require strong assistance. The inside of the caves features hundreds of stairs and is not accessible to wheels.

Common scams to know

There are no traditional tourist scams here. Your biggest risk is financial over-payment: accidentally purchasing individual section tickets rather than the comprehensive all-inclusive combination ticket, or neglecting to bring your own layers and being forced to buy expensive branded mountain clothing at the summit shop.

See & do

Attractions - the full list

The mountain is divided into three distinct cable car sections. Most visitors spend their day across Sections 1 and 2, which house the iconic lookouts and frozen caverns.

The unmissable few

Five Fingers Lookout

Section 2 (Summit)

A spectacular metal viewing platform shaped like a giant hand that juts dramatically out over a vertical 400-meter drop. Each of the five walkways features a unique design, including a glass floor pane and a baroque picture frame to pose inside. The panoramic view of Lake Hallstatt directly below and the surrounding Salzkammergut mountains is unparalleled.

Included in Lift Ticket45 min hike from station8:40am - 5:00pmOpen Daily
Tip: Walk out early in the morning before the noon haze rolls in and crowd lines form at the tips of the fingers. Watch your step if mountain frost is present.

Dachstein Giant Ice Cave

Section 1 (Schönbergalm)

A mesmerizing frozen underground world filled with massive ice curtains, frozen waterfalls, and towering artistic ice sculptures. The 50-minute mandatory guided tour takes you deep through illuminated stone tunnels, across a suspension bridge, and features an impressive light and music show that brings the ancient glaciers to life inside the rock.

~€45+ (Ticket combos vary)50-min tour9:20am - 4:00pm (Seasonal extension)Closed in Winter
Tip: It sits at a steady -1°C inside. Wear your winter gear and remember there is a steep 15-to-20 minute uphill hike from the Section 1 cable car station just to reach the cave mouth.

Welterbespirale (World Heritage Spiral)

Section 2 (Summit)

A distinctive ship-shaped aluminum viewing platform located on the peak of the Krippenstein summit. A spiraling path leads to the top deck where relaxing wooden sun loungers await. It provides a sweeping 360-degree viewpoint that looks directly across to the snow-covered Dachstein Glacier and down into the valley below.

Included in Lift Ticket15 min hike from station8:40am - 5:00pmOpen Daily
Tip: This is the best place to fully comprehend the geography of the high alpine plateau; use the mounted information boards to identify the distant jagged peaks.

Also genuinely worth your time

Mammut Cave (Mammoth Cave)

Section 1

One of the deepest and longest karst cave systems in Europe. While it lacks the ice of its neighbor, the guided tour reveals vast, echoing underground rock cathedrals and explains how massive prehistoric rivers carved out these structural mountain voids.

Requires combo ticket50 min tourOpen May-Oct

Heilbronn Circular Trail

Section 2 to 3

An easy, expansive high-alpine walking path that winds across the rugged stone landscape. It is well-signposted and safe for casual hikers, taking you past stunted mountain pines, geological anomalies, and unique historical monuments.

Free1.5 - 3 hoursBest on clear days

The Dachstein Shark

Section 3 (Gjaidalm)

A whimsical, giant metal sculpture of a prehistoric shark settled right along the Heilbronn trail. Visitors can climb up a internal ladder inside the shark's belly to peer out of its teeth at the surrounding sea of stone, a nod to the area's ancient seafloor past.

Free30 min from Section 3Open summer only

Tickets & passes

Purchase the "Dachstein All in One-Ticket" or specific multi-section combination tickets at the valley ticket counter. Be highly vigilant about the time of the last Postbus 543 back to Hallstatt, which typically leaves the mountain base between 5:00pm and 6:20pm depending on seasonal schedules; missing it leaves you stranded with a very steep taxi fare.

Do you need a car?

Absolutely not. The regional Postbus 543 drops you right outside the ticket office doors from Hallstatt, rendering a personal vehicle completely redundant for this trip.

Guided or independent?

Go independently. The trails are exceptionally well marked, transport is effortless, and the required cave guides are already included with your base entry tickets anyway.

Getting around once you arrive

Your primary transit network on the mountain consists of the three heavy-gauge cable car sections. Once you step off the platforms, your own two feet do all the work across rugged terrain.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Dining on Krippenstein means comforting, hearty Austrian alpine fare served inside traditional wood-paneled mountain lodges with wide panoramic sun decks.

Midday kitchen closure

Unlike valley restaurants, mountain lodge kitchens generally serve hot food continuously from 11:00am until the last cable car departs, meaning you won't get locked out of a late lunch after hiking.

The Alpine Kasnocken Debate

Kasnocken (Austrian alpine spaetzle pan-fried with mountain cheese and caramelized onions) is the defining dish up here. Purists debate whether the local mountain cheese blends should lean intensely pungent or remain mild and gooey for visiting tourists.

Coffee/drink culture

Order a "Verlängerter" (an espresso lengthened with hot water) alongside a pastry, or reward your hiking efforts with a cold "Almdudler," Austria's iconic sweet alpine herbal soda.

Staying for dinner?

Do not plan to have dinner on the mountain unless you are an overnight guest at one of the alpine lodges; the restaurants shut down completely as soon as the final afternoon cable car runs its descent sequence.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

The high altitude ensures brilliant, harsh sunlight. For the best landscape depth, photograph during early morning or late afternoon when mountain shadows crawl across the valley floors.

  • The Glass Floor on the Five FingersShoot from a low angle looking straight down through your feet to capture the dizzying 400-meter sheer drop below.
  • The World Heritage Spiral LoungersPosition your camera behind the wooden sun beds, framing a subject looking out toward the massive white Dachstein Glacier.
  • The Blue Floodlights of the Ice PalaceInside the Giant Ice Cave, look for the illuminated frozen pathways where deep blue LED light glows directly through thick sheets of crystal ice.
  • The Dachstein Shark MouthHave a companion climb inside the sculpture and take a portrait looking outward through the sharp, metallic frame of the teeth.

Good to know

Austrian mountain culture uses "Du" (the informal version of "you") almost universally when up above the tree line. Don't be surprised if lodge staff address you warmly and informally right away.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • The last Postbus back to Hallstatt leaves early in the evening; if you miss it, you will have to walk down to Obertraun village or call for a rare, expensive private taxi.
  • There are no secure luggage lockers at the summit. Store heavy suitcases back at your Hallstatt hotel or at the main Hallstatt Lahn terminal before heading to the mountain base.
  • The mountain lodges accept credit cards, but alpine satellite terminals occasionally drop connection. Always carry at least €30 in physical cash for emergency food or drinks.
  • Summer mountain storms can form rapidly within 30 minutes. Check the local bergfex mountain weather forecast app before ascending, as high winds will safely shut down the upper cable cars.
  • Paved paths on Krippenstein are steep enough to cause severe knee strain. Consider bringing collapsible trekking poles if you regularly struggle with downhill walking.
  • Children under four enter the cable cars free, but think carefully before bringing them into the Ice Cave; the freezing damp conditions and numerous stairs can trigger severe tantrums.
