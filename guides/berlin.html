Overview

Berlin is a vibrant, sprawling metropolis known for its rich 20th-century history, diverse neighborhoods, and legendary creative energy. The city blends gritty urban grit with vast green spaces, lakes, and world-class cultural institutions. It has a relaxed, open-minded atmosphere where past and future constantly dialogue.

Best time to visit: May to September. Most people spend 3 to 4 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 5 to 7 days with day trips to Potsdam and Spreewald. Pack comfortable walking shoes for the long distances and a light jacket for unpredictable weather or breezy evenings by the Spree.

Before you book

Many museums and major historical sites require or highly recommend booking a specific timed-entry slot online well in advance, especially during the peak summer months.

Accessibility

Most U-Bahn and S-Bahn stations have elevator access, but some historic areas and older buildings still lack ramps or step-free entry.

Common scams to know

Watch out for the shell game street gambling trick around the East Side Gallery and pickpockets operating on crowded public transit lines.

Attractions — the full list

The unmissable four

The Reichstag Building

Mitte

The historic seat of the German parliament features a spectacular modern glass dome designed by Norman Foster, offering panoramic views of the city and a unique look down into the debating chamber below.

Free (registration required)1 to 2 hours08:00 to 24:00Open daily (dome closures apply for cleaning)
Tip: You must register online days or weeks in advance; last-minute spots are rare and require waiting in lines at the service center.

Museum Island

Mitte

A unique ensemble of five world-renowned museums built between 1824 and 1930, housing spectacular treasures like the Pergamon Altar, the Ishtar Gate, and the famous bust of Nefertiti.

Variable per museum or combined pass3 to 5 hours10:00 to 18:00Mondays (most museums)
Tip: Buy a Museum Island all-day pass and start early at the Neues Museum to beat the largest tour groups.

Berlin Wall Memorial

Mitte / Wedding

The official central memorial site extends along Bernauer Strasse, featuring the last preserved piece of the Berlin Wall complete with the grounds behind it, providing a chilling look at the border fortifications.

Free1.5 to 2.5 hours08:00 to 22:00 (outdoor area)Visitor Center closed Mondays
Tip: Climb the viewing platform at the Documentation Center to clearly see the layout of the former death strip.

Brandenburg Gate

Mitte

Berlin's most iconic neoclassical monument, built in the late 18th century as a symbol of peace, which later came to represent the division of the city during the Cold War and its subsequent reunification.

Free30 minutesEarly morning or at sunset when the stone glows and crowds are smaller.
Tip: Walk through the gate toward Tiergarten to find the moving Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe just a block away.

Also genuinely worth your time

East Side Gallery

Friedrichshain

A 1.3 kilometer long open-air gallery painted directly onto a surviving section of the Berlin Wall, featuring over one hundred paintings from international artists.

Free1 hourOpen 24/7; best viewed in daylight.

Topography of Terror

Mitte

An indoor and outdoor history museum built on the site of the former SS and Gestapo headquarters, detailing the history of repression under the Nazi regime.

Free2 hours10:00 to 20:00 daily.

Checkpoint Charlie

Mitte / Kreuzberg

The famous Cold War border crossing point between the American and Soviet sectors, featuring a reconstructed guard house and open-air display boards.

Free30 minutesOpen 24/7; highly commercialized.

Charlottenburg Palace

Charlottenburg

The largest palace in Berlin, offering splendid baroque interiors and expansive royal gardens built for Queen Sophie Charlotte.

Around 12 to 19 Euros2 to 3 hours10:00 to 17:00 or 18:00; closed Mondays.

Walking

Berlin is flat and has excellent sidewalks, but it is physically massive. Walking between major districts is impractical, so you will want to combine walking with public transit.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

From traditional German pubs to vibrant international street food markets, Berlin offers an incredibly diverse culinary landscape that caters to every budget.

The Currywurst vs the Döner Kebab

The city is fiercely divided over its ultimate street food icon. Currywurst, a sliced pork sausage topped with spiced ketchup and curry powder, was invented here in 1949. Meanwhile, the modern Döner Kebab was popularized by Turkish immigrants in West Berlin during the 1970s. Both are essential culinary experiences.

Coffee/drink culture

When ordering coffee, ask for a Milchkaffee if you prefer a classic French style café au lait, or a Filterkaffee for standard black brew. Specialty cafes will recognize standard flat whites and lattes.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Berlin's beauty lies in its dramatic contrasts, where historical monuments sit right next to industrial ruins and colorful street murals.

  • Oberbaum BridgeCapture this double-deck brick bridge at sunset from the banks of the Spree river.
  • Brandenburg Gate at DuskPhotograph from Pariser Platz when the gate is beautifully illuminated against the deep blue sky.
  • Government District ArchitectureLook for stark geometric lines and reflections on the river outside the Paul-Löbe-Haus in mid-afternoon light.
  • East Side Gallery MuralsWalk along Mühlenstrasse in the morning for soft lighting on the famous fraternal kiss mural.
  • TeufelsbergClimb to this abandoned Cold War listening station for panoramic views over the Grunewald forest at golden hour.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Always carry a small amount of cash as some traditional restaurants, bakeries, and smaller kiosks still do not accept credit cards.
  • Berlin public transit runs on an honor system without ticket barriers, but plainclothes inspectors conduct frequent spot checks and issue steep fines.
  • Almost all supermarkets and retail shops are completely closed on Sundays, so plan your grocery shopping accordingly.
  • Renting a bicycle is often the fastest and most immersive way to cross neighborhoods and see the city like a local.
  • When disposing of plastic bottles or glass containers, look for the word "Pfand" as they carry a cash deposit refundable at grocery stores.
  • Stand on the right side of the escalator on the U-Bahn and S-Bahn network to allow hurried commuters to pass on the left.
