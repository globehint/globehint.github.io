Overview

Berlin is a city structurally and culturally defined by its heavy 20th-century history, yet it refuses to stay anchored in the past. It’s a sprawled, raw metropolis where sleek corporate blocks abruptly give way to bullet-scarred brickwork and graffiti-plastered concrete. The vibe here is staunchly casual, uncompromisingly creative, and notoriously late-night, smelling faintly of Döner meat and coal smoke in the winter. Instead of standard European prettiness, Berlin offers an unmatched industrial edge and a profound sense of reinvention.

Best time to visit: May to September for beer gardens and open-air parties. Most people spend 3 to 4 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 6 or 7 days with day trips to Potsdam and Dresden. Wear broken-in flat shoes as Berlin is massive and demands significant walking across uneven pavement.

Before you book

Berlin relies heavily on cash, especially for small businesses, kiosks (Spätis), and street food stalls. Do not assume your credit card will be accepted everywhere, always keep a €20 note handy.

Closed on certain days

Most museums close on Mondays, and virtually all supermarkets, retail shops, and pharmacies are strictly closed on Sundays due to German trading laws. Plan your grocery runs accordingly.

Accessibility

Most U-Bahn and S-Bahn stations feature step-free elevator access, though repairs can cause unexpected downtime. Historic pavements are frequently wide but cobblestoned, which can shake up wheelchair users.

Common scams to know

Watch out for aggressive pickpockets around Alexanderplatz and inside crowded S-Bahn trains. Avoid the "shell game" gamblers on bridges and groups asking you to sign fake charity petitions.

See & do

Attractions - the full list

Every attraction worth knowing about, not just the postcard shots. Major sights get the full writeup with insider tips; everything else is listed below so nothing's left out.

The unmissable four

The Reichstag Building

Mitte

The seat of the German parliament combines neo-Renaissance architecture with a striking, modern glass dome designed by Norman Foster. Walking up the interior spiral ramp offers 360-degree views of the city skyline while looking down into the active legislative chamber below.

Free2 hours08:00 - 24:00None
Tip: Strict security means you must register online weeks in advance. Book a sunset slot to watch the lights come up across Tiergarten.

East Side Gallery

Friedrichshain

A 1.3-kilometer stretch of the original Berlin Wall preserved as the longest open-air heritage gallery in the world. It features over a hundred murals painted by international artists right after the wall fell in 1989, documenting political hope, freedom, and satire.

Free1.5 hours24/7None
Tip: Walk the wall from Ostbahnhof toward Warschauer Straße to escape the heaviest crowds, and avoid buying "authentic" wall fragments from local street vendors.

Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe

Mitte

An immense, sobering grid of 2,711 concrete slabs (stele) of varying heights situated on a sloping field. Walking into the labyrinth creates an intentional, disorienting sense of isolation and unease. The underground information center below provides a deeply moving chronicle of individual victims.

Free1 hour24/7 (Field)Center closed Mondays
Tip: Maintain a respectful demeanor; climbing, jumping, or sunbathing on the grey concrete blocks is strongly condemned by locals and staff.

Museum Island

Mitte

A unique ensemble of five world-renowned museums nestled on an island in the Spree River. This UNESCO World Heritage site anchors treasures spanning thousands of years, most famously housing the legendary bust of Nefertiti in the Neues Museum and the Ishtar Gate in the Pergamonmuseum.

~€12-€15 per museumHalf to Full DayMorning to Late Afternoon
Tip: Parts of the Pergamonmuseum are undergoing massive long-term renovations. Buy a Museum Island 1-Day Pass online to skip ticket counter lines.

Also genuinely worth your time

Tempelhofer Feld

Tempelhof / Neukölln

A monumental, defunct pre-WWII airport converted into a massive public park. Locals assemble here to bike down abandoned runways, wind-skate, grill, and drink beer at sunset.

Free2-3 hoursCloses at dusk

Berlin Wall Memorial (Bernauer Straße)

Mitte / Wedding

The best and most raw place to understand the physical reality of the border division. Features a preserved section of the death strip complete with a watchtower and a poignant outdoor exhibition.

Free2 hours08:00 - 22:00

Topography of Terror

Mitte

An indoor and outdoor history museum constructed on the literal site of the former SS and Gestapo headquarters, detailing the brutal mechanics of the Nazi regime's rise to power.

Free2 hours10:00 - 20:00 daily

Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church

Charlottenburg

A striking historic church heavily damaged by a 1943 air raid, deliberately left standing as a hollowed-out ruin. It is flanked by an exceptionally beautiful modernist concrete tower lined with blue glass.

Free45 mins09:00 - 19:00 daily

Budget note

Berlin hotels charge a 5% local city tourist tax, which is frequently excluded from the base rates shown on booking websites. Expect to pay this directly at check-in.

Walking

While neighborhoods themselves are exceptionally walkable, do not try to walk between them. Berlin is horizontally massive, and crossing from West to East on foot can consume hours of your day.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Berlin's dining scene ranges from exceptionally cheap immigrant-driven street stalls to high-end Michelin spots. It is arguably the easiest city in Europe to eat vegan.

The Döner vs. Currywurst Battle

Berliners fiercely debate their iconic fast foods. The classic Döner Kebab was popularized here by Turkish guest workers, while Currywurst (sliced pork sausage in spiced ketchup dusted with curry powder) is a post-war Berlin classic. Both are unpretentious, ubiquitous, and best eaten standing up.

Coffee/drink culture

When ordering coffee, a simple "Kaffee" yields black filter coffee. If you want a third-wave experience, ask for a "Flat White" or a "Milchkaffee". In summer, order a "Berliner Weisse mit Schuss" which is a sour wheat beer spiked with bright green woodruff syrup.

Nightlife

Berlin's nightlife is legendary, unforgiving, and deeply rooted in electronic music. Most bars don't fill up until midnight, and clubs run non-stop from Friday night to Monday morning.

The most photogenic spots

Berlin's architecture is raw and dramatic. Catch the early morning light to capture the city before the heavy traffic starts moving.

Good to know

Berliners practice a cultural attitude known as "Berliner Schnauze" (Berlin Snout) which is a gruff, deadpan, and sometimes rude exterior. Don't take it personally; it is simply the city's default baseline tone.

Know before you go

Tips & Tricks

  • Leave the fancy clothes home: Berlin's uniform is strictly casual, utilitarian, and heavily black. Wearing flashy designer suits or heels will make you stand out awkwardly.
  • Carry cash everywhere: From market vendors to upscale restaurants, "Keine Kartenzahlung" (no card payment) signs are incredibly common. Always carry paper currency.
  • Do not step in the bike lanes: Red-brick or painted paths on sidewalks are strictly for fast-moving cyclists. Walking in them will get you aggressively shouted at.
  • Mind the Sunday closure: Plan your weekends around the fact that shops close tight on Sunday. Use that day for flea markets, museums, or long park picnics instead.
  • Don't skip the Spätis: These ubiquitous late-night corner kiosks sell cheap beer and snacks. Grab a cold bottle, open it using the counter opener, and drink it on the street like a local.
