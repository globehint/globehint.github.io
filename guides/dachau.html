Start here

Overview

The Dachau Concentration Camp Memorial Site preserves the solemn history of the first Nazi concentration camp, opened in 1933. It stands as a stark, confronting monument to the victims, featuring preserved barracks, the crematorium area, and a vast, quiet roll-call square. Visiting here is an intense, educational, and deeply somber emotional journey rather than a typical sightseeing tourist excursion. The gravel crunches quietly beneath your feet in a space designed entirely for memory, reflection, and historical learning.

Best time to visit: March to May or September to November when temperatures are mild. A Half day is enough to see the highlights - aim to leave Munich by 8:30am to make the most of it and beat the large tour groups. Wear sturdy walking shoes and dress respectfully; much of the site is outdoors on gravel ground with very little shelter from wind, rain, or sun.

Day trip or overnight?

A half-day trip covers the essentials comfortably. There is absolutely no reason to stay overnight near the Memorial Site itself; most travelers return straight to Munich afterward to process the heavy historical weight of the experience.

Before you go

Admission to the memorial site is completely free, and no tickets or advance reservations are required to enter. However, if you plan to take an official 2.5 hour memorial-led walking tour, you should book your ticket online in advance or arrive early at the visitor center, as these frequently sell out.

Closed on certain days

The Memorial Site is open daily from 9:00am to 5:00pm year-round. It is only closed on December 24th. Unlike many standard museums in Germany, it remains open on Mondays.

Accessibility

The site is predominantly flat and step-free, making it accessible to wheelchairs. However, the paths consist largely of coarse, loose gravel which can be physically tiring to push a manual wheelchair across for long distances.

Common scams to know

There are no active scams at the site itself due to its solemn nature. Beware only of unofficial, unauthorized tour guides hanging around Munich Central Station pretending to represent the official Dachau administration.

The unmissable few

The Main Exhibition in the Maintenance Building

Exhibition Hall

Housed in the former camp kitchen and maintenance facility, this extensive permanent museum details the rise of the Nazi regime, the establishment of Dachau as the prototype camp, and the harrowing daily lives and fates of the prisoners through photographs, documents, and artifacts.

Free2 hours9:00am - 5:00pmOpen Daily
Tip: Rent an audio guide at the entrance building first; the narration provides vital context that ties individual exhibit boards together into a cohesive narrative.

Barracks and Roll Call Square

Camp Grounds

The vast, desolate open space where prisoners were forced to stand for hours in all weather conditions. Two barracks have been reconstructed to accurately show the horrific, cramped progression of the wooden bunk beds over the years as overcrowding worsened.

Free45 mins9:00am - 5:00pmOpen Daily
Tip: Walk all the way to the back of the reconstructed barracks to fully comprehend the architectural scale of the original grid containing 34 barracks foundations.

Crematorium Area (Barrack X)

Rear Grounds

Located quietly across a small bridge at the far end of the camp, this area contains the original small crematorium and the larger Barrack X, which includes the disinfection chambers, the morgue rooms, and a gas chamber disguised as a shower room.

Free30 mins9:00am - 5:00pmOpen Daily
Tip: This is the most emotionally distressing section of the site. Maintain strict silence and be aware that children under 13 are not recommended to visit this specific area.

Also genuinely worth your time

The Jourhaus Entrance Gate

Camp Entrance

The primary point of entry for all prisoners arriving at Dachau. It features the iron gate forged with the deceptive Nazi slogan 'Arbeit macht frei' (Work sets you free).

Free15 mins9:00am - 5:00pm

Religious Memorials and Chapels

Rear Grounds

Built behind the barracks row, these include the Catholic Mortal Agony of Christ Chapel, the Protestant Church of Reconciliation, the Jewish Memorial, and a Russian Orthodox Chapel, providing spaces for quiet prayer.

Free30 mins9:00am - 5:00pm

The Camp Prison (Bunker)

Behind Main Building

The small courtyard and block of cells where prominent political figures, resistance fighters, and special prisoners were kept in strict isolation and subjected to brutal torture.

Free20 mins9:00am - 5:00pm

Tickets & passes

Buy an MVV 'Single Journey Ticket' or a 'Zone M-1' Day Ticket from the ticket machines at any Munich station. The day ticket covers both the S-Bahn train ride and the local bus connection seamlessly. The last buses run back to the station well past the camp's closing time.

Do you need a car?

No, a car is completely unnecessary. The train and bus infrastructure connects Munich Central Station to the Memorial Site gates with clockwork efficiency.

Guided or independent?

Easy enough independently using the excellent audio guides. A guided tour is recommended if you want to ask complex historical questions, but it is not needed for navigation.

Getting around once you arrive

Once the bus drops you off outside the main visitor center, the entire memorial site must be navigated strictly on foot.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Given the heavy nature of the site, dining options on-site are intentionally basic, and eating or drinking on the camp grounds outside the cafeteria area is strictly forbidden out of respect.

Midday kitchen closure

The small cafe inside the visitor center serves simple snacks and drinks continuously from morning until late afternoon without a midday break.

The On-Site Dining Situation

Most visitors choose to complete their visit first and then travel back into Munich to eat a proper lunch or dinner, rather than dining adjacent to a memorial site.

Coffee/drink culture

Do not walk around the historical camp grounds holding a coffee cup or soda bottle. Keep all beverages tucked away inside your daypack until you return to the visitor center area.

Staying for dinner?

Almost everyone returns to Munich for dinner. Lingering in the town of Dachau for an evening meal is uncommon for day-trippers.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Photography is fully permitted for personal historical documentation, but taking artistic portraits, stylized selfies, or cheerful poses is deeply inappropriate and disrespectful.

  • The Jourhaus GateA close-up shot of the gate lettering captures the chilling entry experience.
  • The Endless Barracks LinePhotographing down the concrete foundations shows the immense, geometric scale of the camp.
  • International Monument SculptureBest shot from directly in front to capture the raw, jagged texture of the bronze forms.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • The S2 train back to Munich runs frequently, roughly every 20 minutes throughout the afternoon, making it impossible to get stranded.
  • Large backpacks and suitcases are strictly forbidden on the memorial grounds. There are small luggage lockers available inside the visitor center, but they fill up fast.
  • Credit cards are universally accepted at the visitor center for audio guide rentals and books, though keeping a few coins handy for locker deposits is smart.
  • The memorial site is incredibly crowded between 11:00am and 2:00pm when large commercial tour buses arrive; entering right at 9:00am ensures a much quieter experience.
  • Wear thick-soled walking shoes; walking across the sharp, uneven loose gravel grounds for several hours will hurt your feet in thin slip-on shoes or sandals.
  • Children under 13 are allowed on the site, but the graphic historical imagery in the main museum film and crematorium is not recommended for young audiences.
