Overview

Strasbourg is a city with a split identity, having bounced between France and Germany four times in the last two centuries. The result is a unique Alsatian culture where you can wash down pork-heavy choucroute with a crisp Riesling while walking cobblestones that feel straight out of a fairy tale. It is a highly walkable, deeply atmospheric river city dominated by a massive pink-sandstone cathedral and a web of sleepy canals.

Best time to visit: May to October, or December for the markets. Most people spend 2 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 4 days with day trips to Colmar and Château du Haut-Koenigsbourg. Bring flat, sturdy walking shoes to handle the brutal historic cobblestones, and a light rain jacket even in summer.

Before you book

Strasbourg hosts the European Parliament, which meets for four days once a month (usually mid-week). Hotel prices quadruple during these session weeks, and the city fills up completely. Check the European Parliament session calendar online before booking any accommodation.

Closed on certain days

Many traditional winstubs (local taverns) and independent shops are closed on Sundays and Mondays. Municipal museums are typically closed on Tuesdays. Worth checking before building a tight itinerary around a specific day.

Accessibility

The historic center (Grande Île) is flat but heavily cobbled, which can be brutal for wheelchairs and strollers. However, the entire tram network is modern, low-floor, and fully accessible.

Common scams to know

Watch out for pickpockets around the Cathedral and Petite France, especially during the crowded Christmas market. Also, ignore the petition signers near the bridges who pretend to be deaf to extract cash donations.

The unmissable four

Strasbourg Cathedral

Grande Île

A pink-sandstone masterpiece that was the world's tallest building for over two centuries. The astronomical clock inside puts on a show daily at 12:30 pm, and the single, soaring spire dominates the Alsatian skyline.

Free (Cathedral) · €8 (Platform)1-2 hours8:30 am - 5:45 pmNone
Tip: Skip the paid 12:30 pm astronomical clock show unless you love long, dry introductory films; instead, pay the fee to climb the 332 steps to the platform just before sunset for unmatched views over the Black Forest and the Vosges.

La Petite France

Historic Center

A postcard-perfect neighborhood of half-timbered tanners' and millers' houses dating back to the 16th century, built right at water level where the River Ill splits into canals.

Free1-2 hours24/7None
Tip: Walk here at sunrise or late at night when the tour groups have vanished and the water reflects the glowing timber frames in perfect silence.

Barrage Vauban & Ponts Couverts

Petite France

A 17th-century pink sandstone defensive barrier and three medieval towers spanning the canals. The roof of the Barrage Vauban functions as a free viewing terrace.

Free45 mins8:00 am - 7:00 pm (longer in summer)None
Tip: Walk across the Ponts Couverts first, then go up to the panoramic terrace of the Barrage Vauban for the classic photo angle of the towers with the cathedral rising behind them.

Neustadt (Imperial Quarter)

Neustadt

Built by the German Empire after annexation in 1871, this area features grand, imposing Wilhelminian architecture, wide avenues, and the circular Place de la République. It is a stark, fascinating contrast to the medieval French core.

Free1.5 hoursLate afternoon
Tip: Walk from Place Broglie across the bridge to Place de la République to feel the sudden, dramatic shift in architectural scale and political ambition.

Housed in three interconnected old timbered houses, this museum offers an intimate look at historical rural life, clothing, and folklore in Alsace.

Strasbourg Airport (SXB) is small and located in Entzheim, just 9km southwest of the city. The journey into town is incredibly quick and painless.

Walking

The historic center (Grande Île) is small and flat. You can walk from one side to the other in 25 minutes. Wear comfortable, thick-soled shoes to survive the historic cobblestones.

The Strasbourg City Card is digital and can be purchased on your phone via the CTS or tourist office app. It is highly flexible since it lasts a full week for a very low initial cost.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Alsatian food is hearty, pork-heavy, and heavily influenced by German culinary traditions. Portions are massive, and the best places to eat are traditional, wood-paneled taverns called "Winstubs".

Choucroute Garnie vs. Tarte Flambée

Choucroute (sauerkraut cooked in Riesling served with sausages, pork knuckle, and potatoes) is the heavy winter king, but locals debate who makes the crispest Tarte Flambée (a paper-thin dough topped with crème fraîche, onions, and lardons). Purists insist it must be baked over a wood fire and eaten with your hands, never with a knife and fork.

Coffee/drink culture

When ordering coffee, a simple "un café" gets you a strong espresso. If you want milk, ask for "un café au lait" or "un allongé" for a weaker black coffee. In the evening, try a "Picon Bière", a popular local aperitif consisting of draft lager spiked with bitter orange Picon liqueur.

Good to know

Trams stop running around 12:30 am on weekdays and 1:30 am on Friday and Saturday nights. If you miss the last tram, the city is safe to walk, or you can easily hail a taxi or Uber from the central squares.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Strasbourg is a dream for photographers, especially when the low sun reflects off the canals or illuminates the cathedral's pink sandstone.

  • Pont Saint-MartinShoot from the bridge at sunset to capture the half-timbered houses of Petite France perfectly reflected in the still canal water.
  • Rue du Bain-aux-PlantesStand in the middle of this cobbled street in the morning to capture the dramatic, curving timber frames of the 16th-century houses.
  • Barrage Vauban TerraceHead up here at golden hour for a panoramic shot of the Ponts Couverts towers with the cathedral spire towering in the background.
  • Place Benjamin ZixPosition yourself near the water's edge to frame the famous Maison des Tanneurs house with the surrounding weeping willows.
  • Rue de la RâpeLook up from this narrow alleyway right next to the cathedral to capture a dramatic, vertical perspective of the towering pink sandstone facade.

Our route

Our self-guided Strasbourg walking tour

This self-guided route takes you from the bustling modern center, through the medieval heart of the city, and into the romantic canals of Petite France. Roughly 2 hours at an unhurried pace.

4 stops 2 hours unhurried pace 2.5 km flat walking Best started 9:30 am

Start at the grand Place Kléber, the city's beating heart. From there, weave through the narrow medieval alleys toward the jaw-dropping Cathedral Notre-Dame. After marveling at the sandstone facade, walk south toward the River Ill and follow the scenic riverside paths west into the historic Petite France district, finishing at the panoramic Barrage Vauban. Each stop below has a name and short note - just walk to the next one when you're ready.

Good to know

You might occasionally hear older locals speaking Alsatian (Elsässisch), a Germanic dialect. While you do not need to learn it, you will see bilingual French-Alsatian street signs all over the historic center. Always greet staff with a warm "Bonjour" before asking a question, as failing to do so is considered highly rude in France.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Wear thick-soled shoes. The historic cobblestones in the Grande Île are beautiful but unforgiving on thin sneakers or heels.
  • Carry some cash. While cards are widely accepted, some smaller winstubs and market stalls have a €10 or €15 minimum for card payments.
  • Book winstubs weeks in advance. The best traditional restaurants like Le Clou or Chez Yvonne are tiny and fill up completely, especially on weekends.
  • Avoid European Parliament weeks. Check the session calendar before booking your trip, as hotel rates skyrocket when politicians descend on the city.
  • Rent a Vélhop bike. Strasbourg is incredibly flat and bike-friendly. Cycling along the canals is one of the most rewarding local experiences.
  • Buy your Christmas market mug. If visiting in December, you pay a €1 or €2 deposit for a plastic or ceramic cup when buying mulled wine (Vin Chaud). You can return it to any stall to get your deposit back, or keep it as a cheap, authentic souvenir.
