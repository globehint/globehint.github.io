Overview

Dresden, often called the "Florence on the Elbe," is a stunning city in Saxony, Germany, famous for its beautifully reconstructed Baroque architecture. Devastated in WWII, it has risen from the ashes with meticulous restoration, offering a powerful blend of historical grandeur and vibrant modern culture. Expect impressive palaces, world-class art collections, and a relaxed riverside atmosphere.

Best time to visit: May-September for warm weather and outdoor activities, or December for the magical Christmas markets. Most people spend 3-4 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 5-7 days with day trips to Saxon Switzerland and Meissen. The city center is largely walkable, but pack comfortable shoes as you'll cover a lot of ground on cobblestone streets.

Before you book

While Dresden has its own airport (DRS), it's a smaller hub. For international flights, consider flying into larger airports like Berlin (BER) or Leipzig (LEJ) and taking a direct train to Dresden Hauptbahnhof, which can often be cheaper and offer more flight options.

Closed on certain days

Most major museums and galleries in Dresden, including the Zwinger and Residenzschloss collections, are closed on Mondays. Many independent shops and smaller restaurants, especially in the Altstadt, also close on Sundays and Mondays. Always check specific opening hours when planning your itinerary.

Accessibility

The Altstadt (Old Town) is largely flat, making it relatively accessible. However, many historic areas feature cobblestone streets which can be challenging for wheelchairs or those with mobility issues. Public transport (trams and buses) are generally modern and wheelchair-friendly.

Common scams to know

Dresden is a safe city, but like any tourist destination, be aware of pickpockets in crowded areas, especially around major attractions and during festivals. Taxis are regulated and metered, so overcharging is rare, but ensure the meter is running.

See & do

Attractions - the full list

Every attraction worth knowing about, not just the postcard shots. Major sights get the full writeup with insider tips; everything else is listed below so nothing's left out.

The unmissable four

Frauenkirche (Church of Our Lady)

Altstadt

The iconic symbol of Dresden's resilience, this magnificent Baroque church was painstakingly rebuilt after its destruction in WWII. Its stunning interior, soaring dome, and powerful history make it an emotional and architectural marvel. Don't just admire it from the outside; step inside and feel the reverence.

Free (church access), Dome climb 8-10 EUR1-2 hoursMon-Sat 10am-6pm, Sun 12pm-6pm (check for services)Closed for services
Tip: Climb the dome for unparalleled panoramic views of Dresden. Go early in the morning to avoid the biggest crowds, or just before closing for a quieter experience.

Zwinger Palace

Altstadt

A masterpiece of Baroque architecture, the Zwinger is a sprawling complex of pavilions, galleries, and ornate gardens. It houses several world-class museums, including the Old Masters Picture Gallery (Gemäldegalerie Alte Meister) with Raphael's Sistine Madonna, the Porcelain Collection, and the Royal Cabinet of Mathematical and Physical Instruments.

Gardens free, Museums 14-16 EUR (combo tickets available)2-4 hoursTue-Sun 10am-6pmClosed Mondays
Tip: Focus on one or two museums to avoid 'museum fatigue.' The Old Masters Picture Gallery is a must for art lovers, while the Porcelain Collection is uniquely Dresden. The gardens are free to wander and best enjoyed on a sunny day.

Residenzschloss (Royal Palace)

Altstadt

Once the residence of Saxon electors and kings, this imposing palace now houses several important museums. The highlight is the Green Vault (Grünes Gewölbe), a stunning treasury of Baroque jewels and art objects. Other collections include the Turkish Chamber, the Armory, and the Coin Cabinet, offering a deep dive into Saxon history and craftsmanship.

14-24 EUR (depending on collections)2-3 hoursWed-Mon 10am-6pmClosed Tuesdays
Tip: The Historic Green Vault requires timed tickets, which often sell out, especially during peak season. Book online well in advance to secure your spot. If you can't get tickets, the New Green Vault is also impressive and easier to access.

Brühlsche Terrasse (Brühl's Terrace)

Altstadt

Known as the "Balcony of Europe," this elevated promenade offers breathtaking views of the Elbe River, the Augustus Bridge, and the Neustadt skyline. It's a perfect spot for a leisurely stroll, people-watching, and soaking in the city's picturesque charm. You'll find several historic buildings and statues along its length.

Free1 hour (for a stroll)Always open
Tip: Visit at sunset for truly magical views as the city lights begin to twinkle. Grab a coffee or a glass of wine at one of the cafes lining the terrace for a perfect end to your day.

Also genuinely worth your time

Semperoper (Semper Opera House)

Altstadt

One of the most beautiful opera houses in the world, renowned for its exquisite architecture and acoustics. Even if you don't catch a performance, a guided tour is highly recommended to appreciate its opulent interiors and learn about its dramatic history of destruction and reconstruction.

Tours 13 EUR, Performances vary1 hour (tour)Tours daily, check schedule

Fürstenzug (Procession of Princes)

Altstadt

This monumental mural, stretching 102 meters along the exterior of the Stallhof, depicts the lineage of Saxony's rulers. Made from over 23,000 Meissen porcelain tiles, it's a unique and impressive piece of public art that survived the bombing of Dresden almost intact.

Free30 minutesAlways open

Neumarkt

Altstadt

The central square of the Altstadt, dominated by the Frauenkirche. It's the vibrant heart of the rebuilt city, surrounded by elegant Baroque buildings, cafes, and shops. A great place to start your exploration or simply sit and watch the world go by.

FreeVariableAlways open

Albertinum

Altstadt

Part of the State Art Collections, the Albertinum houses two distinct collections: the Gallery of New Masters (19th and 20th century art) and the Sculpture Collection. It's a fantastic counterpoint to the old masters, showcasing German Romanticism, Impressionism, and contemporary works.

12-14 EUR2 hoursTue-Sun 10am-6pm

Budget note

Accommodation prices in Dresden can fluctuate significantly, especially during peak season (summer) and the Christmas market period (late November-December). Booking well in advance, particularly for Altstadt hotels, is advisable to secure better rates and availability.

Walking

The Altstadt (Old Town) is very compact and best explored on foot. You can easily walk between the Frauenkirche, Zwinger, Residenzschloss, and Brühl's Terrace. The Neustadt is also very walkable. Be aware that many historic areas feature charming but uneven cobblestone streets, so comfortable, sturdy footwear is a must.

Where to eat & drink

Dresden's food scene offers a mix of hearty Saxon cuisine in historic settings and international flavors in its more bohemian quarters. Don't be afraid to venture beyond the Altstadt for more authentic experiences.

Eierschecke

This unique three-layered baked cheesecake is a Dresden specialty and a must-try. It features a shortcrust pastry base, a quark cheese layer, and a top layer of egg yolk and butter cream. Opinions vary on the best version, but most local bakeries will offer a good rendition.

Coffee/drink culture

Germany has a strong "Kaffee und Kuchen" (coffee and cake) tradition, especially in the afternoon. You'll find plenty of inviting cafes. Beer culture is also prominent, with many traditional pubs (Kneipen) serving local Saxon and German brews. Don't forget to try a "Radler" (beer mixed with lemonade) on a warm day.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Dresden is incredibly photogenic, especially around its Baroque core. Golden hour (sunrise/sunset) works wonders to highlight the warm sandstone facades.

  • Frauenkirche from NeumarktCapture the iconic dome at dusk when the square's lights illuminate the cobblestones.
  • Brühl's TerraceFrame the Elbe River, Augustus Bridge, and Hofkirche, especially at sunset.
  • Fürstenzug (Procession of Princes)Get close-ups of the intricate Meissen porcelain tiles, best in soft morning light.
  • Zwinger Palace courtyardFocus on the ornate sculptures and fountains, particularly the Nymphenbad, for Baroque detail.
  • KunsthofpassageExplore the colorful, artistic courtyards in Neustadt for quirky, vibrant shots.

Good to know

When addressing strangers or people in service roles, always use the formal "Sie" (pronounced "zee") rather than the informal "du" (pronounced "doo"). It's a sign of respect and avoids awkwardness.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Book timed tickets for popular attractions like the Historic Green Vault and the Frauenkirche dome climb well in advance, especially during peak season, to avoid disappointment.
  • Most museums are closed on Mondays, so plan your itinerary accordingly. Use Mondays for exploring parks, the Neustadt, or taking a day trip.
  • Always validate your tram or bus ticket immediately after boarding or purchasing from a platform machine to avoid hefty fines.
  • While card payments are widely accepted, it's still useful to carry some cash for smaller purchases, market stalls, or some independent cafes.
  • Don't limit yourself to the Altstadt. Cross the Augustus Bridge to explore the vibrant, bohemian Äußere Neustadt for a completely different side of Dresden.
  • If visiting in winter (late November to December), experiencing the Striezelmarkt, one of Germany's oldest Christmas markets, is an absolute must.
