Overview

Divided by the Salzach River, Salzburg feels like a living movie set flanked by sheer mountain faces and topped with an imposing medieval fortress. Famous as the birthplace of Mozart, it wears its classical music identity proudly, but pairs it with an surprisingly unpretentious, centuries-old beer hall tradition. The old town is a compact labyrinth of elegant wrought-iron signs and cobblestone streets that bounce with the echo of horse carriages.

Best time to visit: May to September for pleasant outdoor exploring, or December for magical winter markets. Most people spend 2 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 4 days with day trips to Hallstatt and Berchtesgaden. Pack sturdy walking shoes for the steep fortress paths and an umbrella, as the city is notorious for its sudden, local rain showers known as Schnürlregen.

Before you book

If you are visiting during the famous Salzburg Festival (late July through August), book accommodation and events months in advance. Prices skyrocket and the city becomes exceptionally crowded during these weeks.

Closed on certain days

Austria strictly observes Sunday rest laws. Almost all supermarkets and retail shops are completely closed on Sundays. Pharmacies rotate an on-call emergency service (Apotheken-Notdienst), so there's always at least one open nearby - look for the posted sign or call 1455 to find it. Keep this in mind when planning your itinerary or stocking up on essentials.

Accessibility

The Old Town is flat but entirely cobblestoned, which can be jarring. Hohensalzburg Fortress and the historic catacombs involve steep climbs and steps, but the main fortress courtyard can be reached via a wheelchair-accessible funicular.

Common scams to know

Beware of aggressive ticket sellers dressed up in historical Mozart costumes around the Cathedral. They frequently upsell low-quality, overpriced classical concerts in generic venues rather than the premier historic halls.

See & do

Attractions - the full list

Every attraction worth knowing about, not just the postcard shots. Major sights get the full writeup with insider tips; everything else is listed below so nothing's left out.

The unmissable four

Hohensalzburg Fortress

Festungsberg

One of Europe's largest intact medieval castles dominating the city skyline. It offers panoramic alpine views, royal staterooms, and a complex of history museums showcasing military relics.

Around 14.50 EUR2-3 hours09:30 - 17:00Open daily
Tip: Skip the long ticket lines at the lower funicular station by purchasing the Salzburg Card or buying your entry ticket online ahead of time.

Mirabell Palace and Gardens

Neustadt

A stunning geometric geometric Baroque garden famed for its marble statues, fountains, and its appearance in the Sound of Music. The grand palace features the majestic Marble Hall, which regularly hosts classical concerts.

Free entry1 hour08:00 - 18:00Gardens close at dusk
Tip: Visit early in the morning before 09:00 to enjoy the iconic view looking across the Pegasus Fountain toward the fortress without hundreds of tour groups in your frame.

Mozart's Birthplace

Altstadt

Located inside a vibrant yellow building on Getreidegasse, this apartment museum is where Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart was born in 1756. It exhibits his childhood violin, portraits, and original letters.

Around 14.00 EUR1-1.5 hours09:00 - 17:30Open daily
Tip: Download the official free museum app for the audio guide before entering, as the text descriptions on the walls can feel dry and minimal.

Salzburg Cathedral

Altstadt

An imposing 17th-century Baroque cathedral featuring a magnificent dome, five historic organs, and the bronze font where Mozart was baptized. The pristine turquoise and white interior is visually striking.

Around 5.00 EUR45 minsBest early afternoon
Tip: If you visit at midday, you can often catch brief, impromptu organ demonstrations that let you experience the cathedral's exceptional acoustics.

Also genuinely worth your time

Hellbrunn Palace

Anif

An early Baroque villa legendary for its playful hidden trick fountains designed to soak unsuspecting guests. The extensive grounds also house the original Sound of Music gazebo.

Around 15.00 EUR2 hoursOpen April to November

St. Peter's Abbey and Cemetery

Altstadt

One of the oldest active monasteries in the German-speaking world. The beautifully manicured cemetery is carved directly into the rock face of the Mönchsberg mountain, featuring ancient early Christian catacombs.

Cemetery free, Catacombs around 2.50 EUR1 hour09:00 - 18:00

Getreidegasse

Altstadt

Salzburg's bustling historic shopping street, instantly recognizable by its uniform, intricate wrought-iron guild signs hanging over every storefront, from local boutiques to modern chains.

Free entry1 hourShops close Sundays

Hangar-7

Airport District

An avant-garde, aircraft-hangar shaped glass building owned by Red Bull. It showcases a historic fleet of Flying Bulls stunt planes, Formula 1 racing cars, and rotating modern art exhibits.

Free entry1-2 hours09:00 - 22:00

Budget note

Hotels inside the Altstadt charge a steep premium. If you want to save money, look for family-run guesthouses (Gasthöfe) slightly south in the Nonntal area or just east of the Linzer Gasse district.

Walking

The historic core on both sides of the river is tightly clustered and largely pedestrianized. Walking is by far the most efficient and enjoyable way to explore the Old Town, but watch your step on uneven cobblestones and steep paths up the hills.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Dining here leans heavily into hearty Austrian classics served in wood-paneled taverns, offset by an elegant, historic grand café heritage.

The Salzburger Nockerl Debate

This iconic, sweet egg soufflé is baked to resemble the three snow-capped hills surrounding the city. Opinions differ wildly: some call it a glorious local culinary masterpiece, while others dismiss it as an overpriced, overly sweet plate of baked air. It is always made fresh to order, so prepare to wait at least 20-30 minutes for it to arrive.

Coffee/drink culture

Coffee is a formal ritual here. When visiting a traditional café, waitstaff will serve your espresso on a silver tray accompanied by a small glass of fresh tap water, which is topped with an upside-down spoon to show it is freshly poured.

Salzburg is not a wild clubbing city; nightlife here revolves heavily around historic beer gardens, riverside bars, and sophisticated wine cellars.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Salzburg is incredibly scenic from almost any angle, but specific elevated viewpoints offer the best compositions as the sun sets over the river.

  • Humboldt Terrace on MönchsbergStand here at sunset to capture the classic postcard view of the entire Old Town domes framing the fortress.
  • Mirabell Gardens Pegasus FountainAlign the fountain in the foreground with the distant Hohensalzburg Fortress perfectly framed between the garden hedges.
  • Makartsteg BridgeShoot during twilight to capture the city lights reflecting across the Salzach River through thousands of colorful love locks.
  • Kapuzinerberg ViewpointClimb the steep stone steps from Linzer Gasse to reach the bastions for a dramatic, elevated view looking directly down onto the river bridges.

Good to know

In Austria, saying "Tschüss" (the standard German for goodbye) can occasionally be viewed as overly northern German or foreign. Use the local "Auf Wiedersehen" or a casual "Ciao" instead.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Always keep a 1.00 or 2.00 EUR coin handy; many public restrooms and historical museum lockers require coin deposits.
  • Carry cash. Despite modern point-of-sale systems, smaller traditional bakeries, beer gardens, and sausage stands still strictly refuse card payments.
  • Avoid dining directly on Getreidegasse; prices for standard schnitzels are heavily inflated. Walk a few blocks away into the Nonntal neighborhood for cheaper prices.
  • If buying beer at the Augustiner brewery, you are totally welcome to bring your own picnic food into the outdoor garden halls, a completely legal and beloved local tradition.
  • Check museum schedules carefully if visiting late in the year, as several minor sights reduce their operating hours drastically from November through March.
  • Take advantage of the pristine public drinking fountains scattered across the old town to refill your water bottles with crisp mountain spring water for free.
