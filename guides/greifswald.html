Overview

Greifswald is a charming Hanseatic city on Germany's Baltic coast, often overlooked in favour of bigger names. It beautifully blends medieval brick Gothic architecture with a youthful university vibe. Expect historic market squares, a picturesque museum harbour filled with old sailing ships, and the inspiring landscapes that shaped Romantic painter Caspar David Friedrich.

Best time to visit: June to August for the warmest weather and outdoor activities, though May and September offer pleasant temperatures with fewer crowds. Most people spend 2-3 days here, though you could comfortably stretch to 4-5 days with day trips to Rügen and Usedom. Pack comfortable walking shoes, as the city centre is best explored on foot, and layers for the often breezy Baltic Sea climate.

Before you book

Greifswald is a university town, so accommodation can book up quickly during semester starts, graduation ceremonies, or major summer festivals. Book well in advance if your trip coincides with these times.

Closed on certain days

Like much of Germany, many smaller shops, independent businesses, and some museums in Greifswald close on Sundays and public holidays. Plan your itinerary accordingly, focusing on outdoor activities or larger attractions that might remain open.

Accessibility

The historic centre is largely flat, making it relatively easy to navigate. However, some older streets have cobblestones, which can be challenging for wheelchairs. Public transport (buses) is generally accessible, but check individual museum websites for specific ramp or lift availability in historic buildings.

Common scams to know

Greifswald is a very safe city and not known for specific tourist scams. Standard European travel advice applies: be aware of your belongings in crowded areas and ensure taxi fares are agreed upon if not metered, though unmetered taxis are rare.

Attractions - the full list

The unmissable four

St. Nikolai Dom (Cathedral of St. Nicholas)

Altstadt

The towering brick Gothic cathedral dominates Greifswald's skyline. Its impressive interior is a testament to Hanseatic wealth and craftsmanship, but the real draw is the climb up the tower. From the top, you get panoramic views across the city, the Ryck River, and out to the Baltic Sea.

Free (tower climb: ~€2-3)1-2 hoursMon-Sat 10am-5pm, Sun 12pm-5pm (tower hours vary)None
Tip: Check the specific tower opening hours locally, as they can be seasonal or weather-dependent. Go on a clear day for the best views.

Marktplatz (Market Square)

Altstadt

The beating heart of Greifswald, this historic market square is framed by beautifully restored gabled houses in various colours, anchored by the impressive red-brick Rathaus (Town Hall). It's a fantastic spot for people-watching, grabbing a coffee, or simply soaking in the Hanseatic atmosphere. Regular markets add to the lively scene.

Free1 hour+Always openNone
Tip: Visit early in the morning for quieter photos, or in the late afternoon as the sun illuminates the colourful facades. The weekly market (usually Tuesday and Friday) is great for local produce.

Museumshafen (Museum Harbour)

Wieck

A picturesque ensemble of historic sailing ships, wooden jetties, and charming waterfront buildings along the Ryck River. This working museum harbour is a living testament to Greifswald's maritime heritage. You can stroll along the docks, admire the lovingly restored vessels, and enjoy fresh fish sandwiches from the local stands.

Free1-2 hoursAlways openNone
Tip: Combine a visit here with a walk to the Wiecker Holzklappbrücke, the historic drawbridge, which is just a short stroll away. It's particularly atmospheric at sunset.

Caspar David Friedrich Centre

Altstadt

Step into the birthplace of Germany's most famous Romantic painter, Caspar David Friedrich. This museum offers a fascinating insight into his life, his Greifswald roots, and how the surrounding landscapes inspired his iconic works. It's a must-visit for art lovers and anyone interested in the cultural history of the region.

~€5-71-1.5 hoursTue-Sun 10am-5pm (seasonal variations)
Tip: Look out for special exhibitions or guided tours which can offer deeper insights. After your visit, seek out the real-life locations in and around Greifswald that feature in his paintings, like the Eldena Abbey ruins.

Also genuinely worth your time

Eldena Abbey Ruins (Klosterruine Eldena)

Eldena

These atmospheric Cistercian monastery ruins, set amidst greenery by the Ryck River, are incredibly photogenic and famously inspired Caspar David Friedrich. It's a peaceful spot for a walk and offers a glimpse into medieval history.

Free1-1.5 hoursAlways open

University of Greifswald

Altstadt/University Quarter

Founded in 1456, Greifswald's university is one of the oldest in Germany. Wander through its historic main building, admire the architecture, and feel the youthful energy of the town. The Botanical Garden is also part of the university and worth a visit.

Free (some areas)1 hourCampus always open, specific buildings have hours

Pommersches Landesmuseum (Pomeranian State Museum)

Altstadt

This comprehensive museum covers the history, art, and natural history of Pomerania, the region Greifswald belongs to. It houses an impressive collection, including archaeological finds, medieval art, and works by Caspar David Friedrich.

~€8-102-3 hoursTue-Sun 10am-5pm (seasonal variations)

Fischerdorf Wieck (Wieck Fishing Village)

Wieck

Just beyond the Museumshafen, this charming, well-preserved fishing village offers a glimpse into traditional life. With its small cottages, fishing boats, and proximity to the Baltic Sea, it's a delightful area for a relaxed stroll.

Free1 hourAlways open

Budget note

Greifswald is generally more affordable than Germany's major cities, but accommodation prices can spike during university events (semester starts, graduation) and the peak summer tourist season. Book early for the best rates and availability, especially if travelling between June and August.

Arrive

Getting from the airport

Greifswald doesn't have its own major international airport. The most common arrival points are Rostock-Laage (RLG) for regional flights or Berlin Brandenburg (BER) for international visitors. Getting to the city is straightforward via train or car.

Getting around the city

Greifswald's compact size makes it incredibly walkable, especially the historic centre. For longer distances or trips to Wieck/Eldena, the local bus network is efficient and easy to use with single tickets or day passes.

Walking

Greifswald is an excellent city for walking. The historic Altstadt is compact, largely flat, and many key attractions are within easy reach of each other. Comfortable shoes are a must to explore the cobblestone streets and riverside paths. The walk from the Altstadt to Wieck and Eldena is also very pleasant and mostly flat.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Greifswald's culinary scene is a mix of traditional German fare, fresh Baltic Sea fish, and international student-friendly options. Don't leave without trying some local seafood, especially a Fischbrötchen.

Fischbrötchen (Fish Sandwich)

This is the quintessential North German snack: a fresh bread roll filled with pickled, smoked, or fried fish (often herring, salmon, or mackerel) and sometimes onion or remoulade. It's a simple, delicious, and cheap must-try, especially by the harbour. Locals will passionately debate which stand serves the best one.

Coffee/drink culture

Greifswald's coffee culture is relaxed, with plenty of cozy cafes perfect for lingering. Craft beer is gaining popularity, but traditional German lagers and pilsners are still the go-to. Don't be surprised if your beer comes with a substantial head – it's a sign of a good pour.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Greifswald's blend of Hanseatic history, maritime charm, and natural beauty offers numerous photo opportunities. Golden hour (sunrise/sunset) often brings out the best in the brick architecture and waterfront scenes.

  • MarktplatzCapture the colourful gabled houses and Rathaus from the centre of the square, especially at sunset.
  • Wiecker HolzklappbrückeThe historic wooden drawbridge is iconic; best shot from the riverbank or a boat, ideally when it's opening.
  • Eldena Abbey RuinsFrame the ancient brick arches against the sky, particularly beautiful in morning mist or late afternoon light.
  • MuseumshafenFocus on the masts and rigging of the historic ships, with the water and old town in the background.
  • St. Nikolai Dom Tower ViewThe panorama from the top offers sweeping views of the city's red rooftops and the surrounding landscape.

Good to know

German pronunciation can be tricky, especially the "ch" sound (like in "ich" or "ach"). Don't worry too much about perfection; locals appreciate the effort. When addressing strangers or in formal settings, always use "Sie" (formal 'you') rather than "du" (informal 'you').

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • **Embrace the university vibe:** Greifswald is a young city. Look for student-run cafes, bars, and cultural events, which often offer a more authentic and affordable experience.
  • **Rent a bike:** The city's flat terrain and well-maintained bike paths make cycling an excellent way to explore, especially along the Ryck River to Wieck and Eldena.
  • **Cash is still king in some spots:** While card payments are common, especially in larger establishments, some smaller cafes, market stalls, or independent shops might prefer or only accept cash. Always carry a few euros.
  • **Check opening hours on Sundays/holidays:** Many shops and some attractions will be closed. Plan outdoor activities or visits to larger museums which might have special Sunday hours.
  • **Be prepared for Baltic Sea weather:** Even in summer, the weather can be unpredictable. Pack layers, including a light jacket or windbreaker, as coastal breezes can make it feel cooler than expected.
  • **Don't skip the Fischbrötchen:** These fresh fish sandwiches are a local staple and a delicious, quick, and affordable meal or snack, especially when bought directly from a stand at the harbour.
