Overview

Stralsund, a UNESCO World Heritage city, is a Baltic Sea gem in northern Germany. Its Old Town is a stunning example of Brick Gothic architecture, with towering churches, gabled houses, and a charming harbour. Expect a laid-back maritime atmosphere, fresh seafood, and plenty of history.

Best time to visit: June-August for the warmest weather and outdoor activities, though May or September offer pleasant temperatures with fewer crowds. Most people spend 2-3 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 4-5 days with day trips to Rügen and Darß-Zingst. Pack comfortable walking shoes for the cobblestone streets and layers for the Baltic Sea breezes, which can be cool even in summer.

Before you book

Accommodation in Stralsund, especially in the Old Town, can book up fast during the summer months and public holidays. Plan and reserve your stay well in advance to secure your preferred spot.

Closed on certain days

Many smaller, independent shops and some museums in Stralsund tend to close on Mondays, particularly outside the peak tourist season. Sundays are generally quiet, with most retail closed, though restaurants and major attractions usually remain open. Always check specific opening hours if you're planning a tight itinerary.

Accessibility

The historic Old Town of Stralsund features numerous cobblestone streets, which can be challenging for wheelchair users or those with mobility issues. While major attractions often have accessible entrances, some older buildings may lack step-free access. Buses are generally accessible.

Common scams to know

Stralsund is a very safe city with a low incidence of tourist scams. As with any destination, be aware of your belongings in crowded areas and ensure taxis are metered or agree on a price before starting your journey.

See & do

Attractions - the full list

Every attraction worth knowing about, not just the postcard shots. Major sights get the full writeup with insider tips; everything else is listed below so nothing's left out.

The unmissable four

St. Mary's Church (Marienkirche)

Old Town

This colossal Brick Gothic church once held the title of the world's tallest building in the 17th century. Its sheer scale and intricate brickwork are breathtaking, but the real highlight is climbing the 366 steps to the top of the tower for unparalleled panoramic views over Stralsund, the Strelasund strait, and Rügen Island.

~5 EUR1-1.5 hrsMon-Sat 10am-5pm, Sun 11am-5pm (tower hours vary seasonally)None
Tip: Check tower opening hours in advance, as they can be shorter than the church itself, especially outside of peak summer. Go early in the morning for fewer crowds and clearer views.

Ozeaneum

Harbour Island

A modern, award-winning oceanographic museum and aquarium focusing on the marine life of the Baltic Sea, North Sea, and North Atlantic. Its stunning architecture, interactive exhibits, and impressive aquariums, including a massive tank for oceanic fish, make it a captivating experience for all ages. Don't miss the rooftop terrace for great harbour views.

~18 EUR2-3 hrsDaily 9:30am-6pm (longer in summer)None
Tip: Book your tickets online in advance, especially during holidays or weekends, to avoid long queues. Allow ample time, as there's a lot to see and experience.

Old Town Hall (Rathaus)

Alter Markt

Dominating the Alter Markt, Stralsund's Old Town Hall is one of the most magnificent examples of secular Brick Gothic architecture in the Baltic region. Its ornate facade, with its famous "Schauwand" (display wall) and intricate gables, is a masterpiece. While much of the interior is administrative, the building's exterior and the adjacent arcade are a must-see.

Free (exterior)30 minAlways visibleNone
Tip: Visit the Alter Markt both during the day and in the evening. The floodlit Town Hall at night offers a completely different, magical perspective, perfect for photos.

Wulflamhaus

Alter Markt

Standing proudly next to the Town Hall, the Wulflamhaus is another exquisite example of Stralsund's wealthy merchant past. Built in the late 14th century, this gabled house showcases incredible Brick Gothic detail, from its elaborate stepped gable to its decorative niches. It's a testament to the city's Hanseatic power and often houses a restaurant or cafe.

Free (exterior)15 minBest viewed during daylight
Tip: Look closely at the intricate brick patterns and decorative elements that distinguish it from other buildings in the square. It's a prime example of medieval craftsmanship.

Also genuinely worth your time

St. Nicholas' Church (Nikolaikirche)

Alter Markt

The oldest parish church in Stralsund, dating back to the 13th century. Its interior is richly decorated with medieval art, including a stunning astronomical clock and a grand organ. It offers a quieter, more reflective experience than Marienkirche.

Free (donations welcome)1 hrMon-Sat 10am-5pm, Sun 11am-5pm

Stralsund Museum (Kulturhistorisches Museum)

Old Town

Housed in the former Dominican monastery of St. Catherine, this museum covers the rich history of Stralsund and the region. It features archaeological finds, art, furniture, and exhibits on Hanseatic trade and Swedish Pomerania.

~7 EUR1.5-2 hrsTue-Sun 10am-5pm

Gorch Fock I

Harbour

This majestic sailing ship, a three-masted barque, is a historic landmark in Stralsund's harbour. Built in 1933, it now serves as a museum ship, offering a glimpse into naval history and life at sea. You can explore its decks and learn about its storied past.

~5 EUR1 hrSeasonal hours, usually 10am-4pm

Kütertor

Old Town

One of the two remaining medieval city gates, the Kütertor is a well-preserved example of Stralsund's former fortifications. It's less grand than the Frankentor but offers a tangible connection to the city's defensive past and a picturesque photo opportunity.

Free15 minAlways visible

Bring home

Sanddorn (sea buckthorn) products - This tart, vitamin-rich berry is a regional specialty. You'll find juices, jams, liqueurs, and cosmetics made from Sanddorn in many local shops, especially around the Alter Markt and harbour. A bottle of Sanddorn liqueur or a jar of jam makes a distinctively local gift, typically costing a few to 15 euros.

Budget note

Stralsund's popularity, especially during summer and school holidays, means accommodation prices can climb significantly. Booking several months in advance is highly recommended to secure better rates and a wider choice of hotels or guesthouses, particularly in the Old Town.

Arrive

Getting from the airport

Stralsund does not have its own major commercial airport. Most international visitors fly into Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) or Hamburg Airport (HAM) and connect via train or rental car.

Walking

Stralsund's Old Town is exceptionally walkable, with most major attractions located within easy reach of each other. The terrain is largely flat, but be prepared for charming (and sometimes uneven) cobblestone streets, so comfortable, sturdy footwear is essential. Distances are short, making it ideal for leisurely strolls.

Save money

Are the tourist cards worth it?

Unlike larger German cities, Stralsund does not offer a comprehensive "city pass" or tourist card that combines public transport and multiple attraction entries. Therefore, buying individual tickets for transport and attractions is generally the way to go.

While there isn't a single overarching tourist card, the Ozeaneum and the German Maritime Museum sometimes offer combined tickets or discounts if you plan to visit both. Always check their official websites for current offers.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Stralsund's culinary scene is heavily influenced by its Baltic Sea location, with fresh fish taking centre stage. Beyond seafood, you'll find hearty Pomeranian dishes and classic German fare, often best enjoyed in historic settings.

Fischbrötchen: The ultimate Baltic snack

This simple fish sandwich is an absolute must-try. Typically made with pickled herring (Matjes or Bismarckhering), smoked fish, or fried fish in a fresh bread roll, often with onions and gherkins. The debate among locals is always about which harbour stall or fishmonger serves the freshest and best version.

Coffee/drink culture

Coffee and cake (Kaffee und Kuchen) is a cherished German tradition, widely enjoyed in Stralsund's many cafes, especially in the afternoon. When it comes to alcoholic drinks, try a locally brewed beer from the Störtebeker Braumanufaktur, which has a strong presence in the city.

After dark

Nightlife

Stralsund's nightlife is generally relaxed and low-key, focusing on cozy pubs and restaurant bars rather than bustling clubs. The Old Town and harbour areas offer the most options for an evening drink.

After-hours spots

Stralsund is not known for its vibrant after-hours club scene. Most bars and pubs close around midnight or 1 am, especially on weekdays. If you're looking for late-night dancing, options are very limited and may require checking local event listings.

Good to know

The Old Town is safe to walk at night. Taxis are available, but it's wise to book one in advance if you plan to stay out very late, as they might be less frequent after midnight, especially outside of peak season.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Stralsund's rich Brick Gothic architecture, picturesque harbour, and stunning Baltic light make it a photographer's dream. Golden hour (sunrise or sunset) often brings out the best in the brick facades.

  • Alter Markt with Town HallCapture the iconic Town Hall facade and surrounding gabled houses; best at blue hour or sunset for dramatic lighting.
  • Ozeaneum rooftop terraceOffers panoramic views of the harbour, Old Town skyline, and the Rügen Bridge - great for wide shots.
  • St. Mary's Church towerThe highest vantage point for sweeping city views, especially good for capturing the red roofs and other church spires.
  • Hafeninsel (Harbour Island)Frame the historic Old Town with boats in the foreground, particularly stunning at sunrise over the water.
  • Frankentor (Franken Gate)Focus on the impressive medieval brickwork and the gate's towering presence, best captured from a slight distance.

Good to know

Always greet shopkeepers and restaurant staff with "Hallo" or "Guten Tag" before asking a question. Germans appreciate politeness. When in doubt, use the formal "Sie" (you) rather than the informal "du" with strangers.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Wear comfortable, sturdy shoes. Stralsund's beautiful Old Town is paved with cobblestones, which can be tough on your feet after a day of exploring.
  • Embrace the Fischbrötchen. These simple fish sandwiches are a local staple and a delicious, affordable quick meal. Grab one from a stall by the harbour.
  • Be aware of Sunday and Monday closures. Many smaller shops and some museums close on these days, especially outside of peak season. Plan your itinerary accordingly.
  • Book accommodation early for summer visits. Stralsund is a popular destination, and hotels can fill up and become pricey during the peak summer months.
  • Utilise the train network for day trips. Stralsund is well-connected by regional trains, making day trips to Rügen, Greifswald, and other Baltic coast towns very easy.
  • Look up! The intricate details of Stralsund's Brick Gothic architecture, from gables to decorative elements, are often high up on the buildings.
