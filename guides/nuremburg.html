Overview

Nuremberg, Bavaria's second-largest city, is a captivating blend of medieval charm and profound modern history. Known for its imposing Imperial Castle, picturesque Old Town, and the world-famous Christkindlesmarkt, it also holds a solemn past as the site of Nazi rallies and the Nuremberg Trials. Expect gingerbread, bratwurst, and a deep dive into centuries of German narrative.

Best time to visit: May-September or December. Most people spend 2-3 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 4-5 days with day trips to Bamberg and Rothenburg ob der Tauber. Comfortable walking shoes are a must for exploring the Old Town's cobblestones and castle grounds. Layers are wise, even in summer, as evenings can cool.

Before you book

Many smaller shops and some restaurants close on Sundays. Plan your itinerary accordingly, especially for shopping. Public holidays can also affect opening hours significantly.

Closed on certain days

Most museums close on Mondays. Many independent shops and some restaurants close on Sundays and Mondays. Always check specific opening hours online before building a tight itinerary around a specific day.

Accessibility

The Old Town has many cobblestone streets, which can be challenging for wheelchairs or those with mobility issues. The Imperial Castle involves uphill climbs. Public transport (U-Bahn, tram) is generally accessible, but older buildings may lack lifts.

Common scams to know

Nuremberg is generally a safe city with low crime. Be aware of pickpockets in crowded tourist areas (especially during Christkindlesmarkt) and on public transport. Unsolicited "help" at train stations might lead to demands for payment.

The unmissable four

Nuremberg Imperial Castle (Kaiserburg Nürnberg)

Altstadt

Dominating the skyline, this medieval fortress is the city's most iconic landmark. Explore the Imperial Chapel, the Deep Well, and climb the Sinwell Tower for panoramic views over Nuremberg.

~€7-€82-3 hoursApr-Sept 9am-6pm; Oct-Mar 10am-4:30pmDec 24, 25, 31, Jan 1, Shrove Tuesday
Tip: Climb the Sinwell Tower first for the best orientation of the city before exploring the castle grounds. The Deep Well tour is surprisingly fascinating.

Documentation Center Nazi Party Rally Grounds

Südstadt

A somber but essential visit, located in the unfinished Congress Hall. It details the causes, context, and consequences of the Nazi regime's propaganda and the infamous Nuremberg Rallies.

~€62-3 hoursMon-Fri 9am-6pm; Sat/Sun 10am-6pmPublic holidays (check specific dates)
Tip: Allow ample time for reflection. The outdoor grounds are vast; consider taking a tram (Line 8 or 9 to Doku-Zentrum) or bus to get there. The audio guide is highly recommended.

Old Town (Altstadt)

City Centre

The charming heart of Nuremberg, encircled by its medieval city wall. Wander through cobblestone streets, admire half-timbered houses, and discover hidden courtyards, soaking in centuries of history.

FreeHalf to full dayAlways openN/A
Tip: Start your exploration at Hauptmarkt (Main Market Square) and work your way up to the castle. Don't miss the Beautiful Fountain (Schöner Brunnen) and try to find the "lucky ring."

Germanisches Nationalmuseum

Altstadt

Germany's largest museum of cultural history, housing an impressive collection spanning from prehistory to the present day, including art, sculpture, medieval instruments, and folk art.

~€8-€103-4 hoursTues-Sun 10am-6pm (Wed till 8:30pm)
Tip: It's massive, so pick a few areas of interest rather than trying to see everything. The medieval art and Albrecht Dürer exhibits are highlights. Check for free entry days or times.

Also genuinely worth your time

Albrecht Dürer's House (Albrecht-Dürer-Haus)

Altstadt

The preserved half-timbered house where the famous Renaissance artist lived and worked. Offers a glimpse into 16th-century life and Dürer's art.

~€61-1.5 hoursTues-Fri 10am-5pm; Sat/Sun 10am-6pm (Closed Mon)

St. Lorenz Church (St. Lorenzkirche)

Altstadt

A magnificent Gothic church, one of the most prominent in Nuremberg. Admire its stained glass, impressive altar, and the "Annunciation" by Veit Stoss.

Free (donations welcome)1 hourMon-Sat 9am-5pm; Sun 1pm-4pm (check for services)

Handwerkerhof (Craftsmen's Courtyard)

Altstadt (near Hbf)

A charming mini-village near the main train station, featuring traditional Nuremberg crafts, workshops, and small eateries within the old city walls.

Free to enter1-2 hoursShops Mon-Fri 10am-6pm, Sat 10am-4pm

Memorium Nuremberg Trials (Memorium Nürnberger Prozesse)

Justizpalast

Located in Courtroom 600 of the Palace of Justice, where the main war crimes trials took place. It provides context and information about the trials.

~€61.5-2 hoursWed-Mon 10am-6pm (Closed Tue)

Walking

The Old Town is very walkable, with many attractions close together. Expect cobblestones, especially around the Imperial Castle and older streets, so comfortable, sturdy shoes are essential. Distances between key sights are manageable for most.

Where to eat & drink

Nuremberg's food scene is hearty and traditional, deeply rooted in Bavarian and Franconian specialties. Don't expect cutting-edge gastronomy everywhere, but rather comforting, flavourful dishes that pair perfectly with local beer.

Nürnberger Rostbratwürste

The small, finger-sized grilled sausages are iconic. The debate is how many to eat ("Drei im Weckla" - three in a bun, or a plate of 6, 8, 10, or 12 with sauerkraut/potato salad) and where to find the "most authentic" ones. Everyone has their favourite vendor or restaurant.

Coffee/drink culture

Coffee is typically enjoyed with cake in the afternoon (Kaffee und Kuchen). Beer is a serious business here; Nuremberg has a long brewing tradition, with local dark lagers (Schwarzbier) and red beers (Rotbier) being specialties. Don't just ask for a "beer" generically; specify the type you want.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Nuremberg's medieval architecture, winding rivers, and historic squares offer countless photo opportunities. Golden hour (sunrise/sunset) often provides the best light for the Old Town's warm tones.

  • Nuremberg Imperial CastleBest at golden hour from the Old Town below, or panoramic views from Sinwell Tower.
  • WeißgerbergasseEarly morning for empty cobblestone streets and half-timbered houses, or evening for atmospheric lighting.
  • Hauptmarkt (Main Market Square)During the day for the Beautiful Fountain and Frauenkirche, or December for the Christkindlesmarkt magic.
  • Henkersteg (Hangman's Bridge)From the Maxbrücke bridge for a classic view of the river and half-timbered buildings.
  • St. Lorenz ChurchFrom Königstraße for a grand facade shot, or inside for intricate Gothic details.

Our self-guided Nuremberg walking tour

This self-guided walking tour takes you through the medieval heart of Nuremberg, from the bustling market square up to the Imperial Castle, revealing centuries of history and charming hidden corners. Roughly 2-3 hours at an unhurried pace.

4 stops 2-3 hours unhurried pace ~3 km mostly flat with an uphill climb Best started 9-10am

Start at the vibrant Hauptmarkt, soak in the market atmosphere and admire the Frauenkirche. From there, weave through the picturesque Weißgerbergasse towards the river, crossing the Henkersteg. Continue through the charming streets of the Old Town, passing St. Sebald Church, before ascending to the majestic Imperial Castle for panoramic views and a historical deep dive. Finish with a well-deserved beer and pretzel near the castle.

Good to know

Germans appreciate directness and politeness. Always use "bitte" (please) and "danke" (thank you). Formal "Sie" is generally safer than informal "du" when addressing strangers. Pronouncing "ch" as a soft, guttural sound (like in "Bach") is a common challenge for English speakers.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Comfortable shoes are non-negotiable: The Old Town's cobblestones and the castle's uphill paths will be unforgiving on inadequate footwear.
  • Cash is still king in some places: While cards are widely accepted, smaller cafes, market stalls, and some traditional pubs may prefer or only accept cash. Always carry some euros.
  • Sunday closures are real: Many shops, especially independent ones, are closed on Sundays. Plan your shopping or museum visits for other days to avoid disappointment.
  • Christmas Market crowds: If visiting in December, be prepared for immense crowds at the Christkindlesmarkt. Go early in the morning or on weekdays for a slightly less intense experience.
  • Franconian beer culture: Don't just ask for "a beer." Nuremberg and Franconia have specific beer styles like Rotbier (red beer) and Schwarzbier (dark lager). Try a local brew from one of the Hausbrauereien (house breweries).
  • Book ahead for popular restaurants: Especially for dinner on weekends, popular traditional restaurants can fill up quickly. A quick call or online reservation can save you a wait.
