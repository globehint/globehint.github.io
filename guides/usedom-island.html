Overview

Usedom is Germany's second-largest island, shared with Poland and famed for holding the national record for annual sunshine hours. The northern coast is a continuous, 42-kilometre strip of fine white sand, backed by pine forests and the opulent, white-washed resort architecture of the imperial era. It is a place of salty, pine-scented air, creaking wooden piers, and wicker Strandkörbe beach chairs. While the island can feel sleepy off-season and crowded in July, its easy rail connection to Greifswald makes it an effortless coastal escape.

Best time to visit: May to September. A Full day is enough to see the highlights - aim to leave Greifswald by 8:30am to make the most of it. Bring layers even in summer, as the Baltic wind can turn biting without warning, and pack comfortable walking shoes or activewear if you plan to rent a bicycle.

Day trip or overnight?

A day trip covers the classic three imperial resorts (the Kaiserbäder) comfortably if you stick to the eastern end of the island. However, if you want to split your time between the historical rocket sites of Peenemünde in the west and the beaches in the east, or cross the border into the Polish city of Świnoujście, staying one night is highly recommended to avoid spending half your day on regional trains.

Before you go

If you are driving, check the bridge opening schedule for the Peene Bridge in Wolgast. The bridge opens five times a day for 30 minutes to let boats pass, during which car traffic halts completely, creating massive bottlenecks on the B111 approach.

Closed on certain days

While the beaches and piers never close, many independent boutiques and smaller cafes in the interior villages shut on Mondays and Tuesdays, especially outside the peak summer months of July and August. Most supermarkets on the island are closed on Sundays, though select bakeries and souvenir shops open for limited hours under tourist resort exceptions.

Accessibility

The beach promenades in Ahlbeck, Heringsdorf, and Bansin are wide, flat, and fully paved, making them highly wheelchair-friendly. Most modern piers have ramp access, but accessing the actual sand requires using designated mat-lined beach access points (look for the "barrierefrei" signs).

Common scams to know

Watch out for aggressive parking enforcement in the resort towns; private operators ticket cars within minutes of a ticket expiring. On the beaches, seagulls are incredibly bold and will dive-bomb your hands to steal fish rolls - keep your food covered.

The unmissable few

Ahlbeck Pier (Seebrücke)

Ahlbeck

Built in 1898, this is the oldest preserved wooden pier in Germany. Unlike modern concrete structures, Ahlbeck retains its historic charm with a red-roofed pavilion, four green corner towers, and a wooden boardwalk stretching 280 metres into the Baltic. It is the definitive visual icon of Usedom.

Free30 mins24/7None
Tip: Stand on the beach just to the left of the pier at sunrise or golden hour to capture the classic silhouette of the pavilion against the water.

Bäderarchitektur (Resort Architecture)

Heringsdorf & Bansin

During the 19th century, Berlin's elite built opulent summer villas along Usedom's coast. This unique style combines classicist columns, art nouveau balconies, and baroque towers, all painted in brilliant white. Walking the continuous beach promenade between Heringsdorf and Bansin is like strolling through an open-air architectural museum.

Free1.5 hours24/7None
Tip: Walk one block inland from the Heringsdorf promenade to Delbrückstraße to see the most extravagant private villas away from the main beachfront crowds.

Peenemünde Historical Technical Museum

Peenemünde

Located in the northern tip of the island, this former power station was the birthplace of the modern space age and military rocketry. Here, Wernher von Braun and his team developed the V-2 rocket. The museum offers a sobering, brilliant look at the engineering triumphs and the horrific forced-labour cost of the Nazi rocket programme.

€102-3 hours10:00 - 18:00None (May-Sep)
Tip: Climb to the roof of the old power station building for a panoramic view of the Peene River and the vast, abandoned testing grounds.

Also genuinely worth your time

Streckelsberg Cliff Lookout

Koserow

A 60-metre-high active cliff that offers the most dramatic coastal views on the island. A steep but short forest trail leads to the top, where you can look out over the narrowest point of Usedom.

Free1 hour24/7

Wasserschloss Mellenthin

Mellenthin (Interior)

A beautifully restored Renaissance moated castle located in the quiet, forested heart of the island. It features its own microbrewery, coffee roastery, and a peaceful courtyard perfect for escaping the coastal winds.

Free entry1 hour12:00 - 21:00

Sand Sculpture Exhibition

Ahlbeck Border

An enormous covered exhibition featuring incredibly detailed, larger-than-life sand sculptures created by international artists, with themes changing annually. Located right near the Polish border.

Getting there & around

Getting to Usedom from Greifswald is highly straightforward, with regional trains linking the university town directly to the island's coastal rail network. No advanced booking is required.

Tickets & passes

Buy the Mecklenburg-Vorpommern-Ticket (MV-Ticket). It costs €29 for one traveller (and roughly €8 for each additional passenger up to five). It covers your round-trip regional trains from Greifswald and offers unlimited travel on all UBB trains across Usedom for the day. The last train back to Greifswald typically leaves Ahlbeck around 9:30pm.

Do you need a car?

No. The Usedomer Bäderbahn (UBB) train line runs parallel to the entire northern coast, stopping at every major beach resort. A car is only useful if you want to explore the remote southern forests or the quiet backwaters of the Achterwasser.

Guided or independent?

Do this trip completely independently. The train connections are seamless, the resort towns are highly walkable, and there are no complex ticketing systems or skip-the-line bookings to worry about.

Public bathroom access

Public toilets (WC) are located every few hundred metres along the beach promenades. They are generally very clean but require a €0.50 or €1.00 coin to enter, so always keep some loose change in your pocket.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Dining on Usedom revolves around fresh Baltic seafood. While the grand promenades are lined with upscale hotel restaurants, the best culinary experiences are found at rustic beach huts and fish-smoke houses.

Midday kitchen closure

Unlike major German cities, many traditional sit-down restaurants on Usedom close their kitchens between 2:00pm and 5:30pm, even if the venue remains open for coffee and cake. Stick to beachside smokehouses or fish counters if you are eating a late lunch.

The Fischbrötchen Debate

The ultimate local staple is the Fischbrötchen (fish roll). Locals are fiercely divided over the classic pickled Bismarck herring versus the hot, freshly smoked butterfish or matjes. For the absolute freshest version, bypass the fancy promenade cafes and buy directly from the wooden cutters or smokehouses parked right on the sand.

Coffee/drink culture

In the afternoon, join the locals for "Kaffee und Kuchen" (coffee and cake). On Usedom, the drink of choice is Heißer Sanddorn - a hot, thick, sweet-and-sour sea buckthorn juice that is packed with Vitamin C. If the wind is cold, ask for it with a shot of rum.

Staying for dinner?

It is worth staying on the island for dinner if you are in Heringsdorf. Head to Marc's Restaurant for a modern, creative take on regional ingredients, then catch a late train back to Greifswald. If you prefer a quick return, skip dinner on the island and eat back in Greifswald's university quarter where prices are significantly lower.

The most photogenic spots

Usedom's visual appeal lies in its contrast of stark, white imperial architecture against the deep blue of the Baltic Sea and the muted greens of the coastal pine forests.

  • The Ahlbeck Pier ClockShoot from the beach looking up at the historic Art Nouveau clock standing in front of the pier pavilion.
  • Villa Oehler (Bansin)Capture the classic resort architecture lines of this pristine white villa from the beach promenade.
  • The Koserow Salt HutsPhotograph these historic, thatched-roof wooden huts nestled in the dunes, which were once used by fishermen to store salt.
  • The Achterwasser ReedsHead to the lakeside village of Balm at sunset for a serene shot of wooden piers cutting through golden reeds.

Good to know

In Northern Germany, the greeting "Moin" is not just for mornings. You can say it to a shopkeeper at 9:00pm and it is perfectly polite. Saying "Moin Moin" is often considered overly chatty by the famously stoic locals - keep it to a single, friendly "Moin."

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • The last direct train connection back to Greifswald leaves the island around 9:30pm. If you miss it, you will face an incredibly expensive taxi ride across the Wolgast bridge.
  • There are no luggage lockers at the smaller island train stations like Ahlbeck or Bansin. If you have bags, you must store them at Greifswald Hauptbahnhof before boarding the train.
  • Cash is still king at rustic beachside fish shacks. While hotels and major restaurants accept cards, smaller fish-smoke houses and public toilets only take cash. Carry at least €20 in coins and small bills.
  • During July and August, the main beach resorts are heavily crowded. If you want peace, take the UBB train to the smaller stops like Ückeritz or Zempin, where the beaches are backed by quiet pine forests instead of hotels.
  • Always pack windproof clothing. Even on a hot, sunny July afternoon, the Baltic wind blowing off the sea can be surprisingly strong and cold.
  • You must pay a local resort tax (Kurtaxe) of around €2.00 to €3.00 per day to access the beaches. This is usually paid at automated ticket machines near the beach entrances, or included in your train ticket if staying overnight. Keep the paper slip on you as wardens do spot-check.
