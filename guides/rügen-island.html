Overview

Rügen, Germany's largest island, is a captivating blend of dramatic natural beauty and elegant seaside charm. Known for its iconic chalk cliffs, expansive sandy beaches, and picturesque resort towns like Binz and Sellin, it offers a refreshing escape. The island's fresh sea air, historic architecture, and unique narrow-gauge railway give it a distinctive, timeless feel.

Best time to visit: May to September. A Full day is enough to see the highlights - aim to leave Stralsund by 8:00 AM to make the most of it. Bring comfortable walking shoes for exploring the towns and potentially uneven trails in Jasmund National Park.

Day trip or overnight?

A day trip covers the essentials comfortably, allowing you to see the chalk cliffs and a seaside town or two. However, if you want to explore more remote areas like Cape Arkona, enjoy a leisurely evening meal, or simply soak in the resort atmosphere without rushing, staying one night opens that up too.

Before you go

During peak season (July-August), Jasmund National Park's Königsstuhl visitor centre can get very busy. Consider booking tickets online in advance, especially if you plan to visit the exhibition.

Closed on certain days

Most attractions, shops, and restaurants on Rügen are open daily during the main tourist season (May-September). Outside of this, some smaller businesses or specific attractions (like the Königsstuhl National Park Centre) may have reduced hours or close on Mondays or Tuesdays. Always check specific opening times.

Accessibility

Seaside promenades in towns like Binz and Sellin are generally flat and accessible. However, accessing the Königsstuhl chalk cliffs involves walking trails, some of which can be uneven or have stairs, although there is a barrier-free Skywalk (Königsweg) for panoramic views. The historic Rasender Roland train has limited accessibility.

Common scams to know

Germany is generally very safe with few tourist scams. The main thing to be aware of is ensuring you understand parking rules if driving, as fines can be steep. Always use official ticket vendors for attractions and transport.

The unmissable few

Königsstuhl & Jasmund National Park

Stubbenkammer

The iconic chalk cliffs of Königsstuhl are the absolute highlight of Rügen. This dramatic natural wonder offers breathtaking views over the Baltic Sea and the ancient beech forests of Jasmund National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage site. The new Königsstuhl Skywalk (Königsweg) provides an accessible, thrilling perspective.

€12-15 (Skywalk)2-3 hours9am-6pm (seasonal)None
Tip: The Skywalk allows for a great view without needing to descend to the beach. For a classic view *of* Königsstuhl, walk the Hochuferweg (high shore path) from Sassnitz or Hagen to the National Park Centre.

Binz Pier & Promenade

Binz

Binz is Rügen's most elegant seaside resort, famed for its stunning "Bäderarchitektur" (resort architecture) villas and a long, sandy beach. Stroll along the lively promenade, admire the historic buildings, and walk out onto the 370-meter-long pier for panoramic views of the coast and the resort town. It's the perfect spot for a leisurely lunch or coffee.

Free1-2 hoursAlways openNone
Tip: Look for the historic lifeguard tower (Rettungsturm) at the northern end of the promenade, a unique architectural landmark designed by Ulrich Müther.

Rügensche BäderBahn (Rasender Roland)

Southeastern Rügen

Step back in time aboard the "Racing Roland," a historic narrow-gauge steam train that chugs charmingly through the resort towns of southeastern Rügen. Connecting Putbus, Binz, Sellin, Baabe, and Göhren, it's not just transport but an attraction in itself, offering scenic views and a nostalgic experience. A great way to hop between towns.

€8-15 (single trip)30-60 min per segmentSeasonal, check scheduleNone
Tip: If you're travelling between Binz and Sellin, taking the Rasender Roland is a scenic and fun alternative to the bus. Buy tickets directly on the train or at stations.

Also genuinely worth your time

Sellin Pier (Seebrücke Sellin)

Sellin

Another magnificent pier, Sellin's Seebrücke is arguably the most beautiful on Rügen, featuring an ornate Wilhelminian-style pier house and a diving gondola. It's a fantastic spot for photographs and a leisurely walk.

Free (gondola extra)30-60 minAlways open

Baumwipfelpfad Rügen (Tree-top path)

Prora

Located near Prora, this accessible tree-top walk culminates in a unique eagle's nest observation tower, offering stunning 360-degree views over the beech forests, Prora, and the Baltic Sea. Great for all ages.

€12-151.5-2 hours9:30am-6pm (seasonal)

Prora Documentation Centre

Prora

Explore the history of the colossal "Colossus of Rügen," a Nazi-era holiday resort project. The documentation centre provides a sobering look into its origins, construction, and post-war uses, offering a fascinating historical counterpoint to the island's natural beauty.

€8-101-2 hours10am-5pm (seasonal)

Arrive

Getting there & around

The journey from Stralsund to Rügen is very straightforward, thanks to the Rügen Bridge and Causeway. The most common and convenient way to get there is by regional train, which offers direct connections to several key towns on the island.

Tickets & passes

Consider the "Mecklenburg-Vorpommern-Ticket" (MV-Ticket) if travelling by train with 2+ people, as it offers unlimited regional train and bus travel for one day across the state. It's often cheaper than individual tickets for a group. Purchase at train stations or online. The last realistic train back to Stralsund from Binz is usually around 9-10 PM, but always check the current schedule on Deutsche Bahn (DB) for your specific date.

Do you need a car?

No, not strictly. The train connects you to key towns like Binz and Sellin, and local buses (VVR) can take you to Jasmund National Park from Sassnitz. However, a car offers significantly more flexibility to explore the entire island, including more remote spots like Cape Arkona, on a single day.

Guided or independent?

Rügen is very easy to explore independently using public transport, especially if you focus on the eastern side (Binz, Sellin, Jasmund). A guided tour mainly buys you convenience, commentary, and access to a wider range of sites without worrying about logistics, which can be appealing if time is tight or you prefer not to drive.

Getting around once you arrive

Rügen's main resort towns are very walkable, with promenades and pedestrian zones. For longer distances or reaching Jasmund National Park, local buses and the historic steam train are your best bets. The local bus network (VVR) is quite comprehensive.

Public bathroom access

Public toilets are available in larger resort towns (Binz, Sellin, Sassnitz) and at major attractions like the Königsstuhl National Park Centre, often for a small fee (€0.50-€1). Train stations usually have facilities. Cafés and restaurants are also a fallback if you're a customer.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Rügen's food scene is dominated by fresh fish from the Baltic Sea, often smoked or pan-fried, alongside hearty German fare. On a day trip, aim for a satisfying lunch and don't miss out on the local specialties.

Midday kitchen closure

While less common in bustling resort towns, some smaller, traditional restaurants might have a short break between lunch and dinner service, typically between 2:30 PM and 5:00 PM. Most tourist-focused spots, especially along promenades, will offer continuous service during peak season.

Fischbrötchen: The quintessential Rügen snack

The "Fischbrötchen" (fish roll) is the undisputed king of fast food on the Baltic coast. It's a simple bread roll filled with various types of fish, most commonly Bismarck herring (pickled), Matjes (soused herring), or smoked fish like mackerel or salmon. Opinions differ on which fish and which vendor makes the best, but it's a must-try for a quick, authentic bite.

Coffee/drink culture

The coffee and cake (Kaffee und Kuchen) tradition is strong in Germany, and Rügen is no exception. Most cafés offer a wide selection of delicious cakes and pastries. You'll also find plenty of beer gardens (Biergarten) in the summer for a refreshing local brew.

Staying for dinner?

If you have the time and don't mind a later return to Stralsund, lingering for dinner can be a lovely experience, especially in Binz or Sellin. Many restaurants offer fresh seafood and a more relaxed evening atmosphere. Consider a sunset dinner at a pier restaurant. Just be mindful of the last train/bus schedules back to Stralsund.

The most photogenic spots

Rügen offers a diverse range of photographic opportunities, from dramatic natural landscapes to charming architectural details. Golden hour (early morning or late afternoon) often provides the best light for coastal shots.

  • Königsstuhl SkywalkCapture the sweeping views of the chalk cliffs and Baltic Sea from the accessible platform.
  • Binz Promenade & PierFrame the elegant Bäderarchitektur villas against the sea, especially from the pier or beach.
  • Sellin PierThe most ornate pier, best photographed from the beach looking back at its grand structure.
  • Rasender RolandCatch the historic steam train chugging through the landscape, particularly around resort towns.
  • Cape Arkona LighthousesCapture the iconic lighthouses and the rugged coastline at Rügen's northern tip (requires a car for a day trip).

Good to know

The formal "Sie" (you) is used with strangers and in service situations, while "du" is for friends and family. When in doubt, always use "Sie." A polite "Entschuldigung" (en-SHOOL-dee-goong) for "excuse me" or "sorry" is also very useful.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Always double-check the last train or bus service back to Stralsund. Missing it could mean an expensive taxi ride or an unplanned overnight stay.
  • Bring comfortable walking shoes. Even if you plan to use public transport, exploring the towns and the National Park involves a fair bit of walking.
  • While card payments are common, it's wise to carry some small euro notes and coins (€0.50-€2) for public toilets, small purchases, or if a smaller café only accepts cash.
  • Rügen is a popular destination. During peak summer months (July-August) and public holidays, expect crowds, especially at Königsstuhl and in Binz. Arriving early helps.
  • Pack layers. The Baltic Sea weather can be unpredictable; even on a sunny day, a cool breeze can pick up. A light rain jacket is always a good idea.
  • If you plan to visit the Königsstuhl National Park Centre, consider buying tickets online in advance, especially during high season, to save time queuing.
