Overview

Located just north of Berlin in the town of Oranienburg, Sachsenhausen was built in 1936 as a model concentration camp. It served as a training ground for the SS and a site of immense human suffering, later repurposed as a Soviet Special Camp after WWII. Visiting the site is a somber, silent experience defined by stark gravel courtyards, preserved brick barracks, and cold historical reality.

Best time to visit: Year-round, though spring and autumn offer milder weather for walking the exposed outdoor grounds. A half day is plenty to thoroughly view the exhibitions - leave Berlin by 9:00am to ensure a focused, unhurried visit. Wear supportive walking shoes and check the forecast, as the open camp grid provides very little shelter from wind, rain, or summer heat.

Day trip or overnight?

A half-day trip is more than enough to fully engage with the memorial site. There is no reason to stay overnight in Oranienburg, as the town itself was heavily industrial and mostly serves as a quiet commuter suburb today.

Before you go

The memorial operates on a policy of quiet reflection and education. It is highly recommended to rent an audio guide at the visitor center or download the official memorial app ahead of time to understand the layout, as text signs inside are heavily factual rather than narrative.

Closed on certain days

During the winter season (late October to late March), the indoor museum exhibitions and the visitor center are closed on Mondays, though the outdoor camp gates remain accessible daily. Plan your trip from Tuesday to Sunday for the complete experience.

Accessibility

The main roll-call grounds and paths consist of packed, coarse gravel that can be highly uneven and physically taxing for manual wheelchair users. Most reconstructed barracks have ramp options, but help may be needed due to historic door lips.

Respectful Etiquette

This is a burial and memorial site. Avoid loud conversations, phone calls, or posing for stylized photos anywhere on the grounds. Certain zones prohibit filming entirely out of respect for victims.

See & do

Sights within the Memorial

The memorial is structured around the unique triangular layout of the original camp. Main exhibits occupy historic buildings, highlighting separate chapters of the site's dark history.

The essential points of reflection

Tower A & Main Gate

Entrance

The primary command tower and entrance point for prisoners, featuring the iron gate bearing the cynical slogan. It looks directly out over the massive, geometric semicircular roll-call area.

Free30 mins8:30am - 6:00pmNone
Tip: Stand near the base of the tower to observe the architectural design, built specifically so a single machine gun could cover the entire assembly area.

Barracks 38 & 39

Jewish Museum

Two meticulously rebuilt wooden barracks that detail the harrowing daily life, systemic discrimination, and liberation of the camp's Jewish prisoners between 1936 and 1945.

Free45 mins8:30am - 6:00pmClosed Mon (Winter)
Tip: The basement sections contain original structural remains and quiet alcoves for deeper reading.

Station Z

Execution Site

Located outside the central triangular grid, this structure marks the foundations of the purpose-built execution trench, gas chamber, and crematorium ovens used for mass murder.

Free30 mins8:30am - 6:00pmNone
Tip: A modern protective roof covers the ruins, creating a quiet space that serves as the primary wreath-laying and memorial zone.

Also genuinely worth your time

The Camp Prison (Zellenbau)

Internal Jail

A T-shaped isolation block used to punish prominent political prisoners, resistance fighters, and church figures in complete isolation.

Free30 mins8:30am - 6:00pm

Soviet Special Camp Exhibit

Post-WWII History

A separate modern museum wing profiling the years 1945 to 1950, when the Soviet secret service used the camp to hold political dissidents.

Free45 mins8:30am - 6:00pm (Closed Mon in Winter)

The Pathology Building

Medical Wing

The stone cellars where medical experiments were conducted on prisoners and where bodies were stored prior to cremation.

Free20 mins8:30am - 6:00pm

Tickets & passes

Oranienburg is located within Berlin's public transit Zone C. You will need a standard Berlin ABC single ticket or a 24-hour ABC Day Ticket. Validate the paper ticket before boarding your train. Make sure to alight at Oranienburg Station, not the smaller Sachsenhausen station stop.

Do you need a car?

No. Public transport drops you within a brief walk or short local bus hop of the main visitor gates, making a car entirely redundant.

Guided or independent?

An independent trip using the official €3.50 audio guide lets you process the heavy content entirely at your own pace, which many visitors prefer over a group tour format.

Getting around once you arrive

Once you exit Oranienburg station, the memorial site is situated just under two kilometers away and can be navigated on foot via well-marked streets.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

There are no dining facilities or cafes within the memorial grounds themselves out of respect. For food, plan to eat near Oranienburg station or head back into Berlin.

Dining Etiquette

Eating meals or picnicking inside the camp gates is strictly prohibited. If you brought small personal snacks or water bottles, consume them discreetly outside the visitor center complex.

For a quick bite near the station

Options around the station are standard, utilitarian commuter choices perfect for a quick espresso or sandwich before heading back to Berlin.

Staying for dinner?

Most visitors head directly back into central Berlin for dinner. The heavy emotional nature of the site makes returning to the city's lively neighborhoods a more natural option for evening plans.

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • The regional RE5 train only runs once an hour, so check timetables carefully on your return step to minimize station wait times.
  • Small token coin fees of €0.50 apply for public restroom use inside the main visitor information center.
  • Audio guides cost €3.50 and are available immediately at the desks without requiring advanced web booking.
  • Children under 12 are generally discouraged from visiting due to the graphic nature of the historical documentation on display.
  • Bring a refillable water bottle during summer, as the completely open terrain can leave you dehydrated with zero shade coverage.
