Overview

Aachen is a city where heavy-duty European history meets a massive, beer-drinking student population. It is famous for its jaw-dropping 1,200-year-old cathedral, sulfurous hot springs that smell faintly of boiled eggs, and a geographical quirk that lets you walk to Belgium and the Netherlands for lunch. The vibe is unpretentious, historic, and highly walkable, free of the glossy pretension of larger German cities.

Best time to visit: May to September for warm beer gardens, or December for one of Germany's most atmospheric Christmas markets. Most people spend 1 to 2 days, though you could comfortably stretch to 4 days with day trips to Monschau and Maastricht. Bring sturdy, flat-soled shoes; the historic center is paved with uneven, ankle-twisting cobblestones.

Before you book

Aachen has no commercial airport. You will be arriving by train, likely via Cologne, Düsseldorf, or Brussels. Book your Deutsche Bahn (DB) or Eurostar tickets well in advance to avoid steep last-minute fares.

Closed on certain days

Most shops and supermarkets are strictly closed on Sundays. Museums are closed on Mondays. Many traditional restaurants close on either Sunday evening or Monday, so check ahead before planning dinner.

Accessibility

The historic Altstadt is highly challenging for wheelchair users due to historic, deep-grooved cobblestones and steep inclines up to the Cathedral and Lousberg. Ponttor and Pontstraße are flatter but still bumpy. Buses are modern and low-floor.

Common scams to know

Watch out for pickpockets around the crowded Elisenbrunnen and during the busy Christmas Market (Weihnachtsmarkt). Taxi drivers occasionally try to take longer routes from the Hauptbahnhof to hotels; insist on using the meter.

See & do

Attractions - the full list

Every attraction worth knowing about, not just the postcard shots. Major sights get the full writeup with insider tips; everything else is listed below so nothing's left out.

The unmissable four

Aachen Cathedral (Aachener Dom)

Altstadt

The oldest cathedral in Northern Europe, built by Emperor Charlemagne around 796 AD. The central octagonal dome (Palatine Chapel) is a masterpiece of Byzantine-Germanic architecture, lined with stunning golden mosaics. Charlemagne himself is buried here, and his marble throne sat 31 German kings.

Free (€2 for photos)1 hour7:00 AM - 7:00 PMCloses early for services on weekends
Tip: Skip the long queue by visiting early in the morning (before 10:00 AM) or booking a guided tour of the upper gallery to see Charlemagne's throne up close, which is otherwise off-limits to general visitors.

Carolus Thermen

Stadtgarten

Aachen's modern homage to its ancient Roman bathing culture. Fed by the mineral-rich, warm waters of the local Rosenquelle spring, this massive thermal bath complex features indoor and outdoor pools, steam rooms, and an extensive textile-free sauna world.

€16 - €223-4 hours9:00 AM - 11:00 PM dailyOpen Daily
Tip: Renting towels and robes on-site is expensive. Bring your own, along with flip-flops, and note that the upstairs sauna area is strictly textile-free (naked), which is the standard German spa etiquette.

Aachen City Hall (Rathaus)

Markt

Built in the 14th century on the ruins of Charlemagne's palace, this grand Gothic-Baroque building dominates the market square. Inside, the Coronation Hall features dramatic 19th-century frescoes depicting scenes from Charlemagne's life, alongside replicas of the Imperial Regalia.

€61 hour10:00 AM - 6:00 PMClosed during official receptions
Tip: Check their website before visiting; the Rathaus is still the active seat of local government and frequently closes for official receptions, weddings, or the annual Charlemagne Prize ceremony.

Elisenbrunnen

Altstadt

A neoclassical pavilion built in 1827 that serves as the symbol of Aachen's spa heritage. Two fountains pump hot, highly sulfurous thermal water (52°C) directly from the Kaiserquelle spring. The smell of sulfur (rotten eggs) is intense but the history is palpable.

Free15 minsMorning, when steam rises dramatically
Tip: Do not drink the water. Despite historical health claims, modern safety signs warn that it contains high levels of arsenic and other minerals, making it unsafe for consumption.

Also genuinely worth your time

Treasury of Aachen Cathedral (Domschatzkammer)

Altstadt

Houses one of the most significant ecclesiastical treasuries in Europe, including the silver-gilt Bust of Charlemagne and the Cross of Lothair.

€51 hour10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (closes 5:00 PM Mondays)

Couven Museum

Altstadt

A beautifully restored 18th-century merchant house showing how Aachen's wealthy elite lived during the Baroque and Rococo eras, complete with a historic pharmacy.

€61 hour10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (Closed Mondays)

Centre Charlemagne

Katschhof

The city's modern museum detailing Aachen's history from its Roman thermal roots through Charlemagne's empire to its industrial decline and university rebirth.

€61.5 hours10:00 AM - 6:00 PM (Closed Mondays)

Lousberg

North Aachen

A 264-meter-high hill offering panoramic views over the city. It features public hammocks, the historic Belvedere tower ruins, and the quirky "Lousberg Legend" bronze statues.

Free1.5 hoursOpen 24/7

Budget note

Aachen is a major university town with over 45,000 students. Avoid visiting during the RWTH graduation weeks (late summer) or during the CHIO equestrian festival (typically June/July), when hotel prices triple and budget rooms sell out months in advance.

Arrive

Getting from the airport

Aachen has no commercial airport. Most travelers fly into Cologne/Bonn (CGN), Düsseldorf (DUS), or Brussels (BRU) and take the train.

1h 45m · ~€35 - €50
Take a local train to Brussels Midi, then catch the high-speed Eurostar (formerly Thalys) or ICE train directly to Aachen. Booking in advance is highly recommended.

Getting around the city

Aachen has no tram or metro system. The city relies entirely on a highly efficient bus network run by ASEAG, alongside e-scooters and bike-sharing.

Walking

Aachen's historic center is highly compact and almost entirely pedestrianized. Walking is by far the fastest and most pleasant way to get around the Altstadt. Just watch your step on the uneven cobblestones and wear supportive, flat footwear.

Aachen does not currently offer a dedicated, high-value "all-inclusive" tourist card like other major cities. Instead, the focus is on regional transit passes and combined museum tickets.

You can purchase the Route Charlemagne Ticket directly at the ticket desk of any participating museum. The transit tickets are best bought digitally via the "naveo" app to avoid dealing with confusing ticket machines.

Eat

Where to eat & drink

Aachen's food scene is a hearty mix of traditional Rhineland pub fare, Belgian-influenced potato culture, and cheap, student-friendly Middle Eastern eats.

The Öcher Sauerbraten Debate

Unlike the standard German Sauerbraten (marinated pot roast), the traditional Aachen version (Öcher Sauerbraten) is sweetened with Aachener Printen (gingerbread) and raisins in the gravy, giving it a rich, sweet-and-sour profile. Historically, it was made with horse meat, and while most places now use beef, traditional taverns like Postwagen still offer the authentic horse meat version for purists.

Coffee/drink culture

Aacheners love their "Kaffee und Kuchen" (coffee and cake). When ordering, try a "Streuselbrötchen" (a sweet, buttery bun topped with sugar crumbles) that is completely unique to Aachen. If you ask for it anywhere else in Germany, they won't know what you're talking about.

After dark

Nightlife

Aachen's nightlife is heavily driven by its massive student population. Don't expect sleek, exclusive nightclubs; instead, the city excels at cozy, beer-soaked pubs, student bars, and alternative venues centered around Pontstraße.

For serious clubbing, many locals actually take the train to Cologne. However, Aachen has a couple of solid, long-standing student clubs that keep the party going locally.

Good to know

Aachen is generally very safe at night. The Pontstraße area is busy and well-lit even at 4:00 AM. If you need a taxi home, they line up near the Ponttor or the Elisenbrunnen. The "ASEAG" night buses run on weekends, but check the schedule on the naveo app as frequencies drop significantly after 1:00 AM.

Frame it

The most photogenic spots

Aachen's architectural contrast between medieval stone, imperial gold, and modern university steel makes it a dream for street photography.

  • Katschhof at DuskStand in the center of the square to capture the glowing Gothic choir hall of the Cathedral on one side and the illuminated City Hall on the other.
  • Elisenbrunnen PavilionPhotograph the neoclassical white columns in the early morning when the warm thermal steam rises against the cool air.
  • Ponttor GateShoot from the grassy park side just before sunset to capture the medieval stone arch framed by ancient trees.
  • Lousberg HammocksWalk up the hill at golden hour for a shot of the colorful giant public hammocks with the entire city skyline stretching out behind them.
  • Fischmarkt AlleysCapture the narrow, cobblestone Krämerstraße looking up toward the towering dome of the Cathedral, ideally on a rainy day when the wet stones reflect the streetlights.

Our route

Our self-guided Aachen walking tour

This self-guided loop takes you through Aachen's Roman roots, imperial monuments, and lively student streets, ending in the heart of the dining district. Roughly 2 hours at an unhurried pace.

5 stops 2 hours unhurried pace 2.5 km mostly flat Best started 10:00 AM

We start at the neoclassical Elisenbrunnen to experience the thermal springs, then wind through the narrow shopping alleys to the Cathedral. From there, we cross the historic Katschhof square to the City Hall, drop down to the historic Fischmarkt, and finish with a stroll down Pontstraße to the medieval Ponttor gate. Each stop below has a name and short note - just walk to the next one when you're ready.

Good to know

The most famous local word is "Tschö" (pronounced "chuh"), which means "Goodbye." It is a direct loan from the French "adieu" via regional dialects, and you will hear everyone from bus drivers to bakery staff use it instead of the standard German "Tschüss."

Know before you go

Tips & tricks

  • Wear flat, supportive shoes. The historic Altstadt is beautiful but paved with highly uneven, ankle-twisting cobblestones.
  • Always carry cash. Despite Germany's slow shift toward card payments, many smaller cafes, bakeries, and traditional pubs in Aachen still strictly enforce a "cash only" or "EC-card only" policy.
  • Try a Streuselbrötchen. This sweet, buttery yeast roll covered in sugar crumble is a protected local specialty. You can buy them at any Nobis bakery.
  • Don't drink the fountain water. The water at Elisenbrunnen is hot and smells of sulfur, but signs clearly state "Kein Trinkwasser" (Not Drinking Water) due to high natural mineral and arsenic levels.
  • Respect the sauna rules. If you visit Carolus Thermen, the sauna area is strictly "FKK" (Freikörperkultur), meaning you must be completely naked. Wearing a swimsuit in the saunas is forbidden.
  • Plan around Sunday closures. Like the rest of Germany, all shops, supermarkets, and pharmacies are closed on Sundays. Plan your shopping accordingly, though some bakeries remain open in the morning.
